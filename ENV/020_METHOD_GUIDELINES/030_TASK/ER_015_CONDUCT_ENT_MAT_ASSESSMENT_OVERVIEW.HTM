ER.015 - CONDUCT ENTERPRISE MATURITY ASSESSMENT

The purpose of this task is to assess the maturity of the enterprise related to a predefined area, for example, to assess the enterprise architecture maturity, the SOA maturity, the Cloud maturity, or some other architectural maturity. The assessment would either be conducted directly with the enterprise, internally within the implementing organization (for example, Oracle) by the account team, or as a self assessment by the enterprise.

Maturity Analysis and Recommendations Report

This work product highlights the enterprise’s maturity in various dimensions and provides an overall rating with a detailed description of the rating in each major category as well as the implementing organization's (for example, Oracle) recommendation for a follow-up engagement.

APPROACH

The goal of this task is to:

  • Ensure that any following engagement (for example, Insight) is aware of the enterprise’s maturity using either organization-specific or industry-standard (for example, TOGAF Maturity Model) architecture maturity models. The resulting insight from the maturity assessment helps ensure that:
    • More relevant questions are asked in any follow on discovery tasks.
    • A more relevant articulation of a solution proposition (that is, the end work product is presented at the appropriate level of depth/complexity).
    • The enterprise’s context is better understood.
    • The implementing/supplying organization (for example, Oracle) has a better understanding of how it can assist the enterprise in moving to its target situation and/or implementing their strategy.
  • Help in obtaining a better understanding of the enterprise's overall maturity based on industry defined or recognized levels of maturity (that is, defined by Industry Standards bodies such as the Open Group) that would:
    • Help to compare the enterprise against their peers.
    • Help to understand how the enterprise can move to a higher level of maturity (as relevant).
    • Help to understand how follow-up projects or engagements could better enable the enterprise achieve their target level of maturity.

Define Rational for Conducting the Enterprise Maturity Assessment

A maturity assessment performed in depth takes time and requires time from key resources within the organization. Therefore, it is important to clearly define the rationale for executing the maturity assessment. The definition should cover the type of results the organization should expect, how the results be used, and the overall benefit be of conducting the assessment.

Having a clear rationale for the maturity assessment is useful when inviting key stakeholders to participate, and can be used during the introduction for each interview. It also increases the likelihood of identifying key stakeholders who are willing and able to find time to participate.

Prepare Scope of the Assessment

It is necessary to have a clear scope definition for the assessment, and the assessment results should reflect that. Performing a maturity assessment covering the whole enterprise can be quite time consuming, but it may also be necessary. It all depends on the objective of the assessment, and how much the organization differs between business lines, domains, countries and regions. If you need to reveal the areas with the largest differences within the enterprise as a whole, you probably need to assess the whole enterprise. On the other hand, if one business line or department is the initiator of a certain initiative and they need to understand their level of maturity, it is probably sufficient to assess that business line or department only. Then if it is to be expanded to the rest of the organization, a larger assessment would be useful.

Agree on the Assessment Methodology

It is important to agree on the assessment methodology before starting the assessment. Below are some examples:

Enterprise Architecture Maturity Assessment

If the maturity assessment is an Enterprise Architecture Assessment, an assessment would normally cover the following dimensions of enterprise maturity:

  • IT Architecture Process
  • IT Architecture Development
  • Business-IT Linkage
  • Senior Management Involvement
  • Line of Business participation (in development of the EA)
  • Architecture Communication (IT Group’s success in communicating the value of EA to the business)
  • IT Security
  • Architecture Governance
  • IT Investment and Acquisition Strategy (i.e., the impact on the EA organization)
Service-Oriented Architecture (SOA) Maturity Assessment

For SOA, the maturity assessments are based on a SOA Maturity Model. The model defines various SOA domains, for example:

  • Business and Strategy
  • Organization
  • Governance
  • Projects, Portfolio and Services
  • Operations, Administration and Management
  • Information
  • Infrastructure
  • Architecture

The typical approach is to have stakeholders from the enterprise answer questions for the various SOA domains and rate the answers against a maturity matrix in order to identify areas for SOA improvement. A typical SOA Maturity Model includes domain definitions, questions relevant for each domain and the maturity matrix. There are many organizations, including Oracle, that have defined SOA Maturity Models and offer SOA readiness assessments as Services. For Oracle, SOA readiness assessments are conducted by an Oracle professional assisting the enterprise in understanding SOA from a strategic perspective.

Cloud Maturity Assessment

For Cloud, the maturity assessments are based on a Cloud Maturity Model. The model defines the same domains as for SOA, and the typical approach is similar to the approach described for SOA above.

Secure Enterprise Stakeholder Sponsorship

Before performing the actual assessment, you must secure the enterprise stakeholders sponsorship, to make certain that you can include all the required resources for the assessment, when they are needed. In particular, if you are traveling to perform different interviews, you need to be certain that all the interviewees can attend when required.

This means that you need to prepare exactly what kind of resources you need for all the assessment areas in scope. Preferably, you should ensure that you have contact persons with sufficient authority to secure the planning and execution of the interviews with the right participants. It must be very clear, that the quality of the result is totally dependent on the quality of the interviews. Without the right people, the investment in completing the assessment is not advised.

Conduct Assessment

When everything is ready, conduct the actual assessment. This typically is executed in the form of interviews with different individuals. It is important that you get the perspective from different kinds of individuals, with different types of roles, coming from different parts of the organization (depending on the scope). It is important to hear differences of opinions. This may be difficult if multiple persons are interviewed simultaneously.

Preferably, start with an introduction presentation for all the interviewees in the same location, where you explain the purpose, rationale, scope and expectations of the assessment. This saves time later during the individual interviews. However, consider a short introduction reminder at the start of each individual interview. In this way, the interviewee can come prepared and bring any documents that are pertinent to the interview.

Two individuals should perform the interviews, where one is responsible for asking the questions, and the other is responsible for documenting the results. Both may need to ask clarifying questions.

When the interviews have been performed, score the enterprise (or the parts of the enterprise) following the guidelines of the maturity assessment tool being used. This should allow you to identify stronger and weaker areas. Summarize the results into a Maturity Analysis and Recommendations report, and create a executive-level presentation including the high-level findings and recommendations.

Present Maturity Analysis and Recommendations Report to the Enterprise

When the assessment is complete and you have completed the recommendations, present the results to the enterprise stakeholders. Use a presentation covering the high-level findings and recommendations, and point to the report for the details.

Agree on Follow Up Engagement with Enterprise

End the presentation with a summarized number of recommendations for the enterprise. Suggest a meeting with one or a few of the participants to agree on a follow up. A typical follow up includes a more detailed look at the recommendations as well as prioritizing the recommendations in order to provide input to developing a roadmap allowing the enterprise to move from the current state to the desired future state.

WORK PRODUCT DETAILS

Audience, Distribution and Usage

The Maturity Analysis and Recommendations Report is used in the following ways:

  • to obtain an objective view of the organization's current situation related to the subject being assessed
  • to obtain an understanding of which areas are the most urgent to address
  • as an input to determine the desired future state, within a given timeframe, and a specific roadmap to get there

Distribute the Maturity Analysis and Recommendations Report as follows:

  • to key stakeholders from senior management that are important decision makers in the way forward, preferably through a presentation
  • to participants who have been involved in the assessment
  • to stakeholders who will be involved in further activities to obtain an understanding of the current state
