SERVICE ENGINEERING PROCESS MONITORING

The purpose of this technique is to assist in adopting instrumentation for the purpose of monitoring and management of the engineering processes.

Engineering is a core discipline within most Information Technology (IT) organizations, and in many instances, instrumentation is already being utilized to collect data on some aspects of the engineering processes. Traditionally, techniques for applying instrumentation against the engineering processes have centered on overall project delivery lifecycle activities, including delivery timeframes, level of effort, and defect tracking.

Engineering, within an IT strategy utilizing a service-oriented architecture (SOA), requires an additional level of discipline with respect to the engineering processes. This is necessary because engineering within a SOA should be considered as finer grained. That is, applications and projects are constructed with service-orientation in mind, which means that reuse and strategic service opportunities are identified during the analysis phase. With this approach, portions of a project or application may be realized by using existing shared services, or carved out for the purpose of creating additional services. Services are then engineered specifically for reuse among a larger audience of consumers, and potentially providing a higher level of strategic importance with respect to business objectives.

When executed correctly, this strategy should lead to a more responsive and efficient software factory. However, because of the decreased granularity and increased demand for discipline, more oversight, monitoring and management is necessary in order to achieve optimal results.

Instrumentation, therefore, should be considered as a fundamental component of the engineering processes of a service-oriented architecture to accomplish a few key goals:

  1. Ensure that the engineering processes are performing as predicted.
  2. Evolve the engineering processes in targeted areas for improved performance and efficiency based on information gleaned through instrumentation.
  3. Use instrumented data to understand the level of maturity attained with respect to overall SOA goals and objectives.

Instrumenting the Engineering Processes for the Purpose of Monitoring and Management

From a planning perspective, there are two primary activities that must take place with respect to adopting instrumentation for the purpose of monitoring and management of the engineering processes.

The first activity is the identification of the data that will be collected during the execution of the engineering processes. This data is considered to be instrumented, as it is specifically collected for the purpose of defining metrics that will assist in improved monitoring and management over the engineering processes. During this activity, it is important to precisely define what data will be collected from each process, in addition to defining the relationship among related instrumentation entities.

The second activity acts on the raw, instrumented, engineering data and defines metrics through aggregation and data analysis. Each metric has a specific purpose and set of guidelines for interpretation. The metrics are used to both monitor the engineering processes to ensure consistency and overall procedural effectiveness, as well as provide guidance from a management perspective as to where improvements in the processes may be needed.

OUM with the SOA Engineering Planning View provides a fine-grained set of engineering processes for delivering projects and applications within a service-oriented architecture. This document provides a foundation for defining the conceptual data set to be collected through instrumenting any engineering framework based on these processes. The conceptual data is then applied in the definition of several key metrics which may be utilized to monitor and manage the adopted engineering framework.

If you are not yet familiar with the service engineering processes as described in OUM, you should review the material in the SOA in OUM views prior to proceeding with this document.

Conceptual Instrumentation Data Relationships

When the service engineering process in OUM is examined from a high-level, the following logical artifacts identify themselves:

  • Project: The service engineering process is based on delivering projects within an SOA, so this artifact is quite obvious.
  • Requirement: Requirements represent the fine grained details with respect to the functional and non-functional aspects of projects (and services).
  • Service Candidate: Service Analysis is focused heavily on Identifying and Discovering services through the use of Service Candidates.
  • Release Schedule Impact: These are identified during the SOA Release Planning activities and are the result of schedule impacts caused by constraints in engineering and/or physical resources.
  • Service Boundary: Service boundaries identify areas within the requirements of a service candidate where separate services should be defined.
  • Service: The engineering of services is the primary focus of Service Delivery.
  • Defect: Defects are identified during service and project testing activities. Defects may also surface during production operations.
  • Trouble Ticket: A trouble ticket is created when a problem is encountered during production.

The relationships are defined as follows:

Metrics

Problems in human-centric processes are sometimes difficult to track down and isolate. Instrumentation, when applied correctly, provides a means for constructing metrics that may be used to assist with isolating problems or inefficiencies within the engineering processes. It should not be expected to solve the problem however, and nor should it be expected to point directly at the problem (at least in most cases). Further, metrics continue to evolve. Metrics may be refined, or decommissioned in addition to adding new metrics. As the business changes, and IT maturity grows, so should the set of metrics change in order to assist the business and IT in monitoring and managing its processes, systems and activities. Metrics need to be defined with a purpose, and, in the case of engineering, targeted at either identifying problems within specific areas of the engineering process, or identifying areas of inefficiencies within the processes that could possibly be optimized. The last three steps described in the Technique Steps below cover a generic set of metrics targeted at measuring and identifying problems and inefficiencies within the service engineering processes.

APPROACH

Iterative Process

The process of defining instrumentation metrics for engineering should be an iterative process as follows:

  • Brainstorm initial set of engineering metrics.
  • Define the conceptual instrumentation data model necessary to support these metrics.
  • Define a metric representation notation.
  • Examine the engineering framework in detail and further define conceptual instrumentation data based on decision points and significant entities (e.g., Service Candidate, etc) encountered in the processes.
  • Define complete set of metrics based on completed conceptual data model.

The steps in the technique section above do not reflect this process, as this technique overview attempts to provide an input to the iterative process, what kind of engineering metrics would be required, and what kind of instrumentation data would be required to support those metrics. When brainstorming an initial set, you would typically do this for one area at a time, for example, start off with metrics and instrumentation data for Service Analysis.

Conceptual Instrumentation Data

The conceptual instrumentation data that you will see constructed throughout this technique is not simply limited to building metrics for the purpose of improving the engineering processes. They may also be utilized for other types of metric collection or even correlated back from metrics related to other areas. For instance, a core component of measuring SOA Maturity is engineering performance. The same data used to narrow down issues and inefficiencies within the engineering processes may also be applied against more coarse grained metrics for gauging the level of engineering maturity achieved within the organization. Further, metrics gathered for operations purposes may be correlated back through data collected in the engineering processes. This is why it is very important to think broadly when planning out the data set with respect to instrumentation data and their relationships. For optimum flexibility, the data collected should map nicely with the output and objectives of the processes and/or environment being measured.

In the following section, many (but not all) of the data elements collected from the engineering processes can be applied in constructing specific metrics related to narrowing down issues within the engineering processes or assisting in understanding areas of inefficiency. Some of the data will be utilized in other areas for the purpose of understanding SOA Maturity levels in addition to applications in operations based metrics. See the Accelerating SOA Maturity technique overview for more information.

Determine Required Data for Instrumenting the Service Analysis Process

Service Analysis consists of three main sets of engineering practices:

  • SOA Requirements Management
  • Service Identification and Discovery
  • SOA Release Planning

These topics are covered in detail in the SOA Engineering Planning view and its associated technique overviews for each area. This section covers the instrumentation of the Service Analysis processes that are described within that view.

SOA Requirements Management

SOA Requirements Management consists of gathering requirements at the project level, followed by refining the requirements and classifying them at the enterprise level. The information gathered within the process provides a key enabler for the rest of the analysis processes. The value of the information gathered through SOA Requirements Management grows over time. The SOA Requirements Management process is illustrated in the figure below:

SOA Requirements Management - Instrumentation

The Service Analysis processes begin with a project. When a project is put forward and the requirements management process begins for the project there are a number of instrumentation data elements that should be collected with respect to the project. These include:

  • Project ID: This is a unique identifier that uniquely identifies the project’s instrumentation record.
  • Timestamp: This represents the date and time that the project was initiated. It is important to capture time sensitive data as it allows for metrics to be constructed by sampling across isolated time boundaries.
  • LOB: This element holds the name or identifier for the Line of Business from which the project was initiated or belongs.
  • Project Owner: This element lists the owner for the project.
  • Budgeted Cost: This element contains the budgeted cost for the project which allows for future analysis on overall project delivery performance.

During the requirements management process requirements are analyzed and examined at the enterprise level. When a new requirement is encountered a new requirement record is created consisting of the following instrumented data elements:

  • Requirement ID: Uniquely identifies this requirements instrumentation record.
  • Timestamp: This represents the date and time the requirement was created and should match that of the originating project’s timestamp.
  • Originating Project ID: This links the requirement back to the project for which it was originally created. Since requirements are treated as enterprise assets within this process a single requirement may be encountered several times by multiple projects. This field allows tracking information to determine many different aspects of how requirements are being introduced within the enterprise.
  • Is Relevant Outside Project Scope: This value is set to true if it is determined during the requirement processing that this requirement is likely to be relevant to other projects. Otherwise, the value is set to false.

As each requirement is processed checks for duplicate or overlapping requirements are examined. In the event that an existing requirement is encountered its Encountered Count element is incremented to track this event.

Service Identification and Discovery

Service Identification and Discovery provides an analytical method for identifying service candidates together with discovering existing services and service candidates. This process is driven from the information gathered through SOA Requirements Management. The result of the process is a set of service candidates that have been justified and will be scheduled for delivery through SOA Release Planning, as well as services and service candidates that have been prescribed for reuse by the project.

The high-level Service Identification and Discovery Process is illustrated below:

Service Identification and Discovery - High-Level Process


In this process, service candidates are proposed from two angles. The first is by iterating through each of the functional activities and examining the state of the requirements to identify reuse candidates, and proposed service candidates. The second is by studying the business entities and proposing data service candidates, or reuse candidates. These reuse candidates are examined further through a boundary analysis, which will give an updated list of service candidates, or reuse candidates.

The shared service candidates are then run through the justification process. Proposed candidates that are justified will be marked for realization and those that are not will be set to inactive. Service reuse candidates are passed through the reuse validation process. In this process, the candidate will be either prescribed for reuse if the project is authorized to use the shared asset or reuse will be denied.

Instrumentation data is collected from each of these processes beginning with the Functional Activity Analysis process, which is depicted below:

Functional Activity Analysis - Instrumentation

This process has three primary branches which are centered on the creation, extension, or discovery of service candidates. In the event that a new service candidate is created a new Service Candidate instrumentation record is created with the following established elements:

  • Service Candidate ID: This is a unique identifier that identifies the service candidate’s instrumentation record. This value should include version information so that candidate versions can easily be correlated. Alternate version schemes are also possible.
  • Timestamp: This represents the date and time that the service candidate was created.
  • Requirements List: This element links the service candidate’s instrumentation record to the associated requirements instrumentation data. This allows metrics to be constructed which aggregate data between related service candidates and their requirements.
  • Originating LOB: This is the Line-of-Business that has put forward the service candidate for justification; this does not mean that the scope of the services resulting from the service candidate will be scoped to the particular LOB.

In the event that a service candidate is encountered where an extension is to be proposed, a new service candidate instrumentation record is created along with the following additional elements that are tracked with respect to the instrumented service candidate data:

  • Is Identified Service Extension: This value is set to true as an existing service candidate is being proposed for extension.
  • Version Change Required: This value is set to major, minor, or point based on the severity of the change required to the service based on the proposed change or addition in requirements.
  • Requirements List: The new or changed requirements are linked through this element. This element links the service candidate’s instrumentation record to the associated requirements instrumentation data. This allows metrics to be constructed which aggregate data between related service candidates and their requirements.

Service Identification and Discovery - High-Level Instrumentation

The discovery of a justified service candidate results in the creation of a Service Discovery instrumentation record:

  • Service Discovery ID: This is a unique identifier that uniquely identifies the discovery event.
  • Service Candidate ID: This is used to correlate the discovery event back to the service candidate.
  • Timestamp: This represents the date and time that the service candidate discovery event took place.

After new service candidates are identified, or existing candidates are discovered, justification processes are utilized to determine if the new service candidates, or candidate extensions should be realized, or if discovered candidates may be reused by the project.

The instrumentation requirements for the Service Candidate Justification process are illustrated below. This process covers the justification of new or proposed service candidates and service candidate extensions for realization as shared services.

Service Candidate Justification - Instrumentation

The Service Candidate Justification process represents a scoring procedure which is used to analyze the benefits of realizing a service candidate as a shared service against the additional costs or impacts required to realize this candidate as a shared asset. The decision results should be captured as instrumented data. In doing so, the following additional elements are captured as part of the Service Candidate instrumentation record:

  • Is Justified: This value is set to true if the result of the process determines that the service candidate, or candidate extension is to be realized as one or more shared services. Otherwise, the value is set to false.
  • Primary Reason ID: This value represents the primary reason for either justifying the service candidate for realization or alternatively, for failed justification. A set of codes should be defined based on each potential result. This allows reasoning data to be correlated.

The following list represents a set of reason codes related to justification:

  • Enablement Ease: This value is provided for the Primary Reason ID when enablement ease is the primary reason for which the service candidate was justified fro realization.
  • Compliance: This value is provided for the Primary Reason ID when compliance benefits that will be realized through the creation of this service candidate as one or more shared services represent the primary reasoning for realization. Compliance is used in the business sense.
  • Agility Enabler: This value is provided for the Primary Reason ID when a particular service candidate is justified for realization based on the amount of anticipated agility based benefits that it will provide back to the business when realized.
  • Reuse Probability: This value is provided for the Primary Reason ID when a particular service candidate is justified for realization based on the anticipated amount of reuse that may result from realization.

When a service candidate fails justification, the following list of reason codes related to failed service candidate justification applies:

  • QoS Feasibility: This value is provided for the Primary Reason ID when service candidate justification failed based on the difficulty of meeting QoS requirements complicated by realizing the service candidate as a shared asset.
  • Project Impact: This value is provided for the Primary Reason ID when the realization of a particular service candidate will have too much of a negative impact on one or more in-flight or operational projects and/or services.
  • Technology: This value is provided for the Primary Reason ID when a particular service candidate fails justification due to a lack of available technology necessary to realize the requirements of the service candidate in a shared environment.
  • Skill Set: This value is provided for the Primary Reason ID when a particular service candidate fails justification due to a lack of required delivery skills necessary to realize the requirements of the service candidate in a shared environment.

The Primary Reason ID value sets provided above represent a default set, and should be customized or extended to meet the particular demands of the adopting SOA.

One final step required for justified service candidates is to set the Is Shared value of each attached requirement’s instrumentation record to true.

When service candidates are discovered and linked to existing shared services (or services in the process of being realized), a validation process is used to determine if a project may indeed reuse the service. The instrumentation requirements for the Reuse Validation process are illustrated below:

Reuse Validation - Instrumentation

The Service Candidate Reuse Validation process represents a cross-check procedure that is used to analyze the appropriate fit for a service with respect to the project’s intended uses. The decision results should be captured as instrumented data. In doing so, the following additional elements are captured as part of the Service Discovery instrumentation record:

  • Is Validated: This value is set to true if the result of the process determines that the service candidate can and should be reused by the project. Otherwise, the value is set to false.
  • Primary Reason ID: This value represents the primary reason for denying a service for reuse (successful validation reasons are not interesting as a service must be validated against all criteria in order to be validated). A set of codes should be defined based on each potential result. This allows reasoning data to be correlated.

The following list represents a set of reason codes related to reuse validation failures:

  • Insufficient Capacity: This value is provided for the Primary Reason ID when the intended amount of reuse expected from the consumer will exceed available system resource capacity.
  • Service Design: This value is provided for the Primary Reason ID when it is determined that the way in which the service has been designed has made the service in appropriate for the intended consumer’s usage requirements.
  • Security Policy: This value is provided for the Primary Reason ID when access to the service is restricted, and the proposed consumer is not authorized to use the service based on predefined security policies.
  • Requirements Mismatch: This value is provided for the Primary Reason ID when further requirements analysis determines that the requirements of the consumer will not be completely met by the service.

Again, the Primary Reason ID value set provided above represent a default set, and should be customized or extended to meet the particular demands of the adopting SOA.

SOA Release Planning

SOA Release planning is an iterative process that schedules SOA projects and service candidates for delivery. The dependencies between projects and service candidate delivery cycles are also managed through SOA Release Planning through to deployment. SOA Release Planning takes care of resource constraints through priority based contingency planning.

The scheduled release dates for projects and service candidates are prime targets for instrumentation as they provide overall performance indicators with respect to engineering efficiency. Further, when resource constraints are encountered decisions need to be made. The decisions made, which lead to project or service delivery impacts, should be instrumented.

Instrumentation data captured from the high-level SOA Release Planning process is depicted below:

SOA Release Planning - High-Level Instrumentation

The SOA Release Planning process coordinates the scheduling of both projects and service candidates that have been justified for realization. As the high-level project schedule is constructed, the final list of requirements assigned to the project will be available. In addition to setting the requirements list, the project priority should be captured as part of the instrumented data. Finally as the process completes, the project’s scheduled release date is determined and should also be captured with respect to instrumentation data. The following elements are affected in this process:

  • Project Priority Score: This is a value representing the projects priority with respect to the enterprise that may be used to determine importance ratings when a project impact is necessary to make room for a higher priority project under resource constraint conditions.
  • Requirements List: This element links the project’s instrumentation record to the associated requirements instrumentation data. This allows metrics to be constructed which aggregate data between related projects and their requirements.
  • Scheduled Release Date: This element captures the estimated or scheduled release date assigned to the project.

Further, for each service candidate passed through the process, the Scheduled Release Date value should also be instrumented.

When resource constraints are encountered, a contingency process is initiated to determine the course of action for handling the constraint. When impacts are assessed against projects or services, the impacts should be recorded from an instrumentation standpoint. Instrumentation of the Contingency Planning process is depicted below:

Contingency Planning - Instrumentation

There are two scenarios that may impact project or service candidate schedules. The first is when a decision is made to reduce the scope of a service candidate in order to free up resources for another service or project. In this event, the service candidate is versioned and a new service candidate instrumentation record is created. The ID, Timestamp, Justification and Reason ID are copied forward from the previous candidate’s instrumentation record. The following values are then set:

  • Scheduled Release Date: This value holds the new release date for the service candidate.
  • Requirements List: The new or changed requirements are linked through this element. This element links the service candidate’s instrumentation record to the associated requirements instrumentation data. This allows metrics to be constructed which aggregate data between related service candidates and their requirements.

The second scenario is when the decision is made to impact one or more project and/or service schedules in order to make room for the higher priority initiative. In this scenario, an instrumentation record is created representing the Release Schedule Impact as follows:

  • Impact ID: This value uniquely identifies the Release Schedule Impact’s instrumentation record.
  • Timestamp: This value is set to the date and time for which the Release Schedule Impact decision was made.
  • Impact Reason Code: This value is set to one value from a set of valid values representing general reasoning for project schedule impacts.
  • Affected Projects: This value is used to correlate the impact to the set of projects impacted. For each project a Cost of Impact value is set representing the cost expected to the project’s schedule.
  • Affected Services: This value is used to correlate the impact to the set of services impacted. For each service a Cost of Impact value is set representing the cost expected to the service’s schedule.

The following list represents a set of reason codes related to release schedule impacts:

  • Service Factory Capacity: This value is provided for the Impact Reason ID when there is not enough delivery capability available within the service factory to complete the service candidate delivery within the expected schedule.
  • Infrastructure Capacity: This value is provided for the Impact Reason ID when it is anticipated that there will not be enough physical resources available in the expected service infrastructure or hosting environment expected to host the service candidate when deployed to production.

Again, the Impact Reason ID value set provided above represent a default set, and should be customized or extended to meet the particular demands of the adopting SOA.

Determine Required Data for Instrumenting the Service Delivery Process

Service Delivery consists of the core delivery engineering activities taking a service candidate and molding it into one or more services. Service candidates that enter Service Delivery have been justified for realization and scheduled for release. Service Delivery begins with Service Definition where the service boundaries and contracts are created for services resulting from the service candidate. Service Definition primarily determines service boundaries as well as the construction of the service contract. Service contracts include the linked requirements coming from the service candidate, but also may be influenced by enterprise and LOB scoped contracts that are appended to every service based on its intended scope.

Service Design then acts upon the service contracts to develop the services’ interfaces. The process of defining a service interface is much more involved than simply coming up with the input and output for the service. Service Design analyzes the contract from the consumer’s perspective, and is influenced by factors such as scope (enterprise, LOB, application, etc), message exchange patterns (MEPs) as well as non-functional requirements such as expected volume, and response time requirements (specified in the contract).

Service Implementation ensures that all aspects of the service contracts are implemented and upheld through the delivery of business logic as well as the deployment to service infrastructure. The implementation must faithfully realize the service contract and interface which are defined through Service Definition and Design. Both functional and non-functional aspects (such as latency, throughput, availability, etc) of the contract must be fully realized. If some aspects of the contract cannot be realized for any reason, then the contract must be updated to reflect the true reality of the service.

Service Testing plays a role throughout the entire Service Delivery cycle. After Service Definition, the test criteria can be established. Once Service Design has completed, the test cases can be written against the interfaces. Finally, during Service Implementation the test cases are executed to ensure expected quality, and adherence to contract is achieved prior to releasing the service into production.

Service Delivery Overview

In some regards, the Service Delivery process parallels that of a full lifecycle project delivery lifecycle and many of the same principals and practices are applicable not only from a methodology perspective, but also from an instrumentation perspective. Service Definition

Service Definition covers the set of activities required to effectively define service contracts from a service candidate. The service contract for a service provides the detailed description of both functional and non-functional characteristics that must be fulfilled by the interface and the implementation in order for a service to be realized.

The justified service candidate provides the input to Service Definition. This process identifies service boundaries, constructs the contracts from the separated requirements, attaches any enterprise specified contracts, and performs a feasibility check to ensure that for each service defined, the technology and resources available will be able to meet the requirements of the contract.

The outcome of Service Definition is one or more service contracts. The Service Definition process with respect to instrumentation is depicted below:

Service Definition - Instrumentation

There are two main elements that are important with respect to instrumenting the Service Definition process. The first is the identification of service boundaries, and the second is the definition of a service via its service contract.

A Service Boundary instrumentation record is created for each service candidate.

Note: A single service boundary represents a division of requirements within a service candidate, and therefore results in the construction of two separate services. Likewise, when two service boundaries are identified then three services are constructed (number of services = number of service boundaries + 1).

The Service Boundary instrumentation record contains the following data:

  • Service Candidate ID: This value uniquely identifies the Service Boundary’s instrumentation record in addition to correlating it back to the originating service candidate. Since boundary analysis is only done once per service candidate version. The two instrumentation records can share the same ID from a conceptual standpoint.
  • Timestamp: This value is set to the date and time for which the boundary analysis took place against the service candidate.
  • Service Boundary Count: This value is set to -1 when the instrumentation record is created, but will have a minimum value of 0 when the process exits as each boundary identified will result in the value being incremented.

For each service boundary encountered the following is recorded against the Service Boundary instrumentation record:

  • Service Boundary Count: This value is incremented by 1 as each service is defined.
  • Reason ID List: The set of predefined reasons correlating to why a service boundary was identified.

As each service is defined the following is recorded against the Service Boundary instrumentation record:

  • Service ID List: The list of services attached to the service candidate is updated as each service is defined.

If no service boundaries are identified for a service candidate, then the Service Boundary instrumentation record will have a service boundary count of 0 and just a single service attached to the list of services defined through this candidate.

When a service boundary is identified, the following list of reason codes are defined to capture the reason for defining the boundary:

  • QoS: This value is provided for the Reason ID when a service boundary is defined based on variations in QoS requirements.
  • MEPs: This value is provided for the Reason ID when a service boundary is defined based on a variation in Message Exchange Patterns stemming from the service candidate’s requirements.
  • Security: This value is provided for the Reason ID when a service boundary is defined based on variations in security requirements.
  • Scope: This value is provided for the Reason ID when a service boundary is defined because requirements cross scoping boundaries.
  • Category: This value is provided for the Reason ID when a service boundary is defined because the requirements span across one or more service categories.

Define Metric Notation Standards

There are several different ways to note metric computations. A few basic requirements are necessary when selecting a notation:

  1. It must be understood at a relatively high-level.
  2. Ambiguity should be avoided.
  3. It should precisely illustrate the computation briefly and clearly.

For the purpose of this document a relatively simple notation has been selected which meets these criteria. In this notation there are two main clauses:

  • Expression: This clause specifies the way data is computed and aggregated for the metric. The result is the data set necessary to display or analyze the result.
  • Data: This clause defines the data and its conceptual source that is utilized within the metric. It illustrates the relationship between the data utilized within the Computation clause with respect to its source within the Conceptual Instrumentation data collected from the Service Engineering processes, which were detailed in the previous section.

For the purpose of describing the notation a sample computation has been illustrated below:

Sample Metric Computation

The top portion of the above illustration represents the title for the metric. The center provides the metric’s expression, and the lower portion represents the data required in the computation.

The Expression Clause

Statement: for each <target> - The for each clause is used to iterate over the <target> entity or entities. for each clauses may be nested.

Statement: where (conditions) - The where clause is used to provide conditions for the for each clause in which it is nested. The (conditions) statement is optional, and if omitted it means that all entities referenced within appropriate level of the expression will be included in the calculation.

Input: t1 & t2 - The starting time and ending time for the computation is represented as t1 (starting time) and t2 (ending time).

Input: sample - If sample is provided as the <target> of a for each clause, then it is providing the time period to break up the results over. For instance if t1 = Jan 1, 2000 and t2=Dec 31, 2007 and the sample is specified as 1 year; then the for each will take 8 iterations (one for each year). This allows for flexibility, as the sample may be set to something finer grained, or even set to the entire time period.

Referencing Variables: -Variables are identified within the Data clause (see below). If a variable references an entity, an element within the entity can be referenced by using the “.” separator. For instance from the example above, referencing the Service Reuse List of the Project entity would be achieved as follows: p.Service Reuse List.

Booleans: - In the expression, it is sometimes necessary to count against a Boolean expression. Therefore, mathematically, when Boolean are used in expressions, 0 is the mathematical representation for false and 1 is the mathematical representation for true.

The Data Clause

The Data clause contains definitions for the list of variables used in the Expression clause, and maps them to the source within the conceptual instrumentation data from which they originated. A variable can be specified against an entire conceptual instrumentation entity, or elements within entities.

Service Candidate Extension Justification Failures Metric

Purpose: This metric is used to get a gauge on the overall reasoning as to why service candidate extensions are failing justification. Service justification failures are rolled up under the following primary reasons:

  • QoS Feasibility: This value is provided for reasoning when service candidate justification failed based on the difficulty of meeting QoS requirements complicated by realizing the service candidate as a shared asset.
  • Project Impact: This value is provided for reasoning when the realization of a particular service candidate will have too much of a negative impact on one or more in-flight or operational projects and/or services.
  • Technology: This value is provided for reasoning when a particular service candidate fails justification due to a lack of available technology necessary to realize the requirements of the service candidate in a shared environment.
  • Skill Set: This value is provided for reasoning when a particular service candidate fails justification due to a lack of required delivery skills necessary to realize the requirements of the service candidate in a shared environment.

These reasons may be customized to best fit the organization adopting SOA.

Computation: This metric provides reason totals for service candidate extensions introduced within the sample period but fail the justification process, preventing them from being realized as shared services.

Analysis: Service Candidate Extension Justification Failures

Interpretation: The main indicator to look for here is a high-number of service candidate extensions failing because of Project Impact this typically indicates that there is a problem with respect to service evolution within the SOA. This could potentially be result of the following:

  • Inflexible service designs that require high-impact or backwards incompatible changes which are costly and hard to justify.
  • Issues with trust in service testing where too much burden is being placed on applications that utilize services, which must go through extensive testing cycles when services change.
Justified Service Version Change Summary

The Justified Service Version Change Summary metric is depicted below:

Justified Service Version Change Summary Metric

Purpose: This metric provides a measure against how healthy service evolution is within the SOA. In general the service version changes possible include:

  • Major: The change has a large impact on the service and possibly on its consumers. These changes typically involve the largest amount of risk. The change is not backwards compatible, and consumers will be expected to change and migrate to the new service version in time.
  • Minor: The change is backwards compatible but may involve some level of risk to consumers. Consumers may be impacted from a testing standpoint.
  • Point: The change is very small with the least amount of risk. Minimal testing, if any should be required by the service’s consumers.

Computation: This metric gathers data on justified service extensions and totals these events by the severity of the change required to the service.

Analysis: Justified Service Version Change Summary

Interpretation: In general a healthy SOA will have services evolving with the least amount of impact to its consumers as possible. Large numbers beside Point and Minor version changes should not be considered alarming. Large numbers with respect to Major revisions should be scrutinized seriously, as these types of changes are typically high-risk and an overall impeder of SOA efficiency as large amounts of testing may be required. The goal of the service factory is to design services in a way that is as flexible and accommodating as possible to change, a large number of major change revisions requires indicates failure in this respect.

Not Justified Service Version Change Summary Metric

  • Major: The change has a large impact on the service and possibly on its consumers. These changes typically involve the largest amount of risk. The change is not backwards compatible, and consumers will be expected to change and migrate to the new service version in time.
  • Minor: The change is backwards compatible but may involve some level of risk to consumers. Consumers may be impacted from a testing standpoint.
  • Point: The change is very small with the least amount of risk. Minimal testing, if any should be required by the service’s consumers.

Analysis: Not Justified Service Version Change Summary

Interpretation: In general the majority of service extension justification failures should originate from perceived major changes to a service. If point or even minor changes are still being rejected by the justification process then a closer look is definitely warranted, as they changes should be expected within an efficiently operating SOA. For further investigation refer to the metric: Service Candidate Extension Justification Failures which should provide insight into why service candidate extensions are not being justified.

Service Creation vs. Reuse Trend

The Service Creation vs. Reuse Trend metric is depicted below:

Service Creation vs. Reuse Trend Metric

Purpose: This metric provides a measurement of how fast the service catalog is growing with respect to services being reused.

Computation: This metric collects the totals for the number of services initiated within the sample, and the number of services reused by projects or services initiated within the sample.

Analysis: Service Creation vs. Reuse Trend

Interpretation: As an SOA emerges from infancy a tendency towards service creation over service reuse should be expected. This is part of the initial investment required in SOA where you must first create before reuse. Overtime, service creation should begin to shrink in relationship to the amount of services consumed. An optimized SOA will tend to have a very large ratio of reuse to creation, which means there is very little impedance to service discovery and reuse validation, nor is there issues holding back service evolution, or capacity; all of which could cause service reuse to decline or grow less rapidly.

Service Creation vs. Reuse Totals

The Service Creation vs. Reuse Totals metric is depicted below:

Service Creation vs. Reuse Totals Metric

Purpose: This metric provides a ratio of service population with respect to service reuse levels. At a minimum every service created should be reused at least once, therefore a larger ratio of services over reuse is definitely considered as very negative.

Computation: This metric computes a total of the number of services and a total for the amount of reuse events in the SOA.

Analysis: Service Creation vs. Reuse Totals

Interpretation: The reuse count should always be greater than the Service count, even in very “young” SOAs. It makes very little sense to create a service without at least one consumer. Unfortunately, this happens in many instances, and when the first consumer does come along, the first revision to the service is quite often necessary.

There is always some level of additional investment required to deliver a service into a shared environment (right-clicking and creating a web-service is not a service!). In doing so, reuse levels greater than one per service, on average, is necessary to get a minimal savings from the engineering overhead. A large ratio of reuse to service totals is desirable, as this indicates that the right services are being created, and they are flexible and consumable by a large number of consumers, while not being impeded by lengthy evolution requirements, or costly infrastructure upgrades.

Further, if services are typically being justified for realization because of a high-reuse potential, then that trend should be evident here (see metric: Service Candidate Justifications).

Service Boundary Identification

The Service Boundary Identification metric is depicted below:

Service Boundary Identification Metric

Purpose: This metric is used to measure the effectiveness of the Service Candidate granularity resulting from Service Analysis. Typically when Service Candidates are put forward for definition, in an ideal scenario they should already be fine enough grained where minimal service boundaries will be identified. The information gleaned by measuring identified boundaries in relation to service candidates may be used to optimize the SOA Requirements Management and Service Identification and Discovery processes.

Computation: This metric is computed by totaling the number of service candidates encountered in each sample. For each service candidate visited within the sample, the number of service boundaries identified is also counted. This allows for the ratio of service candidates to boundaries to be measured over each sample period.

Analysis: Service Boundary Identification

Interpretation: On average there should be more service candidates than service boundaries. The optimal condition is zero service boundaries. If a relatively high number of service boundaries are being encountered then service candidates are being presented in a format that is too course grained. Getting the granularity of service candidates correct is important. The service justification process works on service candidate data, so if service candidates are being approved which result in many service boundaries then a review of the service justification process may be necessary.

If high numbers are being encountered in this metric, then it may be beneficial to look at reuse levels. If reuse levels are low, in contrast to high service boundary levels, then there is definitely a need to tighten the processes around service candidate identification.

If reuse levels are high in relation to high service boundary levels, then even though course grained candidates are being presented, the services output through delivery still appear to be contributing to the SOA. Therefore this trend should be closely monitored, but no action may be required.

Further, if it is determined that service candidates are too course grained, then the next metric will be useful in determining the primary reasoning trend associated with identified service boundaries. This information may be useful in isolating the area or areas that may need to be addressed with respect to service candidate granularity.

Service Boundary Reason

The Service Boundary Reason metric is depicted below:

Service Boundary Reason Metric

Purpose: This metric provides a summary of totals based on reasons for service boundary creation. The metric data is sampled over a specific period of time which allows the timeframe to be narrowed accordingly. Typically this metric is used for follow-on analysis based on data observed in other metrics (such as the previous metric: Service Boundary Identification). The reasons for identifying service boundaries are as follows:

  • QoS: This value is provided when a service boundary is defined based on variations in QoS requirements.
  • MEPs: This value is provided when a service boundary is defined based on a variation in Message Exchange Patterns stemming from the service candidate’s requirements.
  • Security: This value is provided for when a service boundary is defined based on variations in security requirements.
  • Scope: This value is provided when a service boundary is defined because requirements cross scoping boundaries.
  • Category: This value is provided when a service boundary is defined because the requirements span across one or more service categories.

Computation: This metric is computed by creating a total for each general service boundary reason. Service boundaries are filtered based on the timeframe provided as input to the metric.

Analysis: Service Boundary Reason

Interpretation: A high-number of service boundaries being attributed to any particular reason warrants a closer look. The following provides a summary by reason:

  • QoS: A high value of service boundaries being identified for reason of QoS differences indicates that Requirements Analysis may not be grouping requirements in an optimal way. Typically a high number related to this reasoning is not of large concern; however it may be worth looking at Justification failures. If a large number of service candidates are also failing to be justified based on QoS requirements then there is a high likelihood that viable service candidates are failing justification because they are being bundled with a service candidates with higher QoS expectations that are not applicable across all the requirements.
  • MEPs: Differences in Message Exchange Patterns indicates that requirements may be too course grained. A high number here indicates that some tightening in the SOA Requirements Management process may be necessary to produce finer grained candidates. This may be necessary as candidates may be justified that should not be (or vice-versa) simply because they are grouped with a service candidate that is too course grained.
  • Security: A high number of service boundaries being created based on varying security requirements is typically not a large issue; however it may be worth looking at Justification failures. If a large number of service candidates are also failing to be justified based on Technology or Skill Set impacts then there is a high likelihood that viable service candidates are failing justification because they are being bundled with a service candidates with higher technology or skill set requirements stemming from additional security expectations that are not applicable across all the requirements.
  • Scope: If a high number of service boundaries are being identified due to a variation of scope in requirements then the SOA Requirements Management process definitely requires adjustment as requirements are not being aligned against the functional model correctly.
  • Category: Differences in Category indicates that requirements may be too course grained. A high number here indicates that some tightening in the SOA Requirements Management process may be necessary to produce finer grained candidates. This may be necessary as candidates may be justified that should not be (or vice-versa) simply because they are grouped with a service candidate that is too course grained.

The main exception here is when composite services are being defined during service definition. This does mean that a fairly course grained candidate was presented; however, the additional services created are there to increase flexibility and still service the consumer through the high-level composite service.

Service Architecture Conformance

The Service Architecture Conformance metric is depicted below:

Service Architecture Conformance Metric

Purpose: This metric measures the amount of exceptions with respect to the Service Architecture (part of the Reference Architecture) allowed during service delivery. Exceptions should be accepted on a case by case basis, and represent valid reasons to deviate from the reference architecture in order to meet certain conditions or overcome obstacles encountered during service delivery.

Computation: This metric simply counts the total number of conforming services at the same time as totaling the number of services that do not conform to the service architecture.

Analysis: Service Architecture Conformance

Interpretation: Obviously the lower the number of non-conforming services the better. However there are two sides to this coin. The first is that a high percentage of non-conforming services may indicate problems in the service architecture that need to be addressed. Secondly, a high percentage of non-conforming services may indicate that tightening of the delivery processes may be required; otherwise overall SOA effectiveness may be compromised. After all, the reason standards are defined in the reference architecture is to enhance SOA effectiveness and assist in assuring that the goals of the SOA are achieved.

If a high percentage of non-conforming services are identified then the following metric may be useful to help analyze the situation further.

Service Architecture Non-Conformance Summary

The Service Architecture Non-Conformance Summary metric is depicted below:

 

Service Architecture Non-Conformance Metric

Purpose: This metric measures the general reasoning provided for allowing a service’s delivery to deviate from the service architecture. The following reasons are possible:

  • Schedule: This value is provided when service delivery deviates from the reference architecture in order to meet a schedule constraint.
  • Packaging/Deployment: This value is provided when service delivery deviates from the reference architecture because of packaging and or deployment issues.
  • Security: This value is provided when service delivery deviates from the reference architecture in order to meet specific security requirements.
  • QoS: This value is provided when service delivery deviates from the reference architecture in order to specific QoS requirements.

Computation: This metric simply totals non-conforming services by reason id. The services counted are limited to the provided time parameters to allow for isolation in the data.

Analysis: Service Architecture Non-Conformance

Interpretation: The following provides a summary by reason:

  • Schedule: If a high value is allocated to schedule constraints, then a closer look at the capacity of the service factory, in addition to overall engineering efficiency should be looked at. Conformance deviations related to schedule can be potentially dangerous with respect to overall SOA effectiveness, and in general is a poor reason for deviation from the reference architecture. This value should be very low, and deviations of this kind should be avoided if at all possible.
  • Packaging/Deployment: A high number of deviations for reason of packaging and deployment indicate that it may be worth taking a closer look at the service hosting environments. These types of deviations may impact the overall agility achieved through the SOA, as inappropriately packaging services may create unnecessary dependencies which creates a large impact for service evolution.
  • Security: A high number beside this reason indicates that it may be worth taking a look at enhancing the security technology with respect to the service hosting environment. A high value here may also warrant a look at the Service Candidate Justification Failures metric. If a high number of justification failures is attributed to Technology these justification failures may be due to a lack of security infrastructure, furthering the justification for investment in this area.
  • QoS: A high number beside this reason indicates that it may be worth taking a look at enhancing the QoS capabilities with respect to the service hosting environment. A high value here may also warrant a look at the Service Candidate Justification Failures metric. If a high number of justification failures is attributed to Technology these justification failures may be due to a lack of infrastructure capability related to QoS, furthering the justification for investment in this area.

Production Defects - Trend

The Production Defects - Trend metric is depicted below:

Production Defects - Trend Metric

Purpose: This metric provides a measurement of the level of defects and there severity encountered after a service has been deployed to production. This provides for analysis to be done against the level of quality being achieved through the service factory.

Computation: This metric simply totals the production defects encountered against services in production by severity. The results are grouped by sample as well.

Analysis: Production Defects - Trend

Interpretation: Obviously keeping production defects to a minimum is optimal. Any large number of defects encountered against production services hinders the efforts of the SOA and leads to a lower level of trust between the SOA and its consumers. The severity of defects encountered is also an indicator worth observing. If a large number of high-severity defects are being encountered against production services then a closer look at the testing strategy and processes are definitely in order. The following metric provides additional information regarding the general root cause of the defects which may lead to a better understanding of where improvements are necessary.

Production Defects - Summary

The Production Defects – Summary metric is depicted below:

Production Defects - Summary Metric

Purpose: This metric provides a measurement of the level of defects and there root cause reason, encountered after a service has been deployed to production. This provides for analysis to be done against the level of quality being achieved through the service factory and the type of defects being encountered in production. The following general root cause reasons are measured for defects:

  • Functional: This value is provided for when the defect was caused due to a deviation against functional requirements.
  • Non-Functional: This value is provided when the defect was caused due to a deviation against non-functional requirements.
  • Regression: This value is provided when the defect was caused due to a regression against a previously tested version of the service (or project).
  • Change Control: This value is provided when the defect was caused due to unspecified requirements that are now deemed necessary. (For example, hardware failure, resulting in additional requirements need to be defined that had not previously been considered in scope but are now deemed necessary through Change Control.)

Computation: This metric simply totals the production defects encountered against services in production by reason (generalized by root cause). The results are grouped by sample as well.

Analysis: Production Defects - Summary

Interpretation: The following provides a summary by reason:

  • Functional: If a large percentage of the defects encountered in production against a service are considered functional, then additional scrutiny or emphasis on the functional testing strategy and processes may be warranted.
  • Non-Functional: If a large percentage of defects encountered in production against a service are considered non-functional, then additional integration and system testing may be necessary to prevent an increasing trend in these types of defects.
  • Regression: When a large number of defects are recognized as being regressions encountered through service evolution, then the level of trust in the SOA is definitely at risk. Additional emphasis on the regression testing processes will be needed to ensure a decent level of trust between consumers and the service factory. If a service factory cannot be trusted to evolve services with minimal impact, then the organization may have to revert back to lengthy test cycles regardless of change type which will severely impact the SOA’s ability to respond to business demands quickly.
  • Change Control: A large value recorded against these types of defects indicates that additional emphasis is required during requirements analysis. These types of defects are most commonly associated with gaps in non-functional requirements which were not considered until a condition arose in production. Every effort should be taken to minimize these types of defects as they may have a large impact with respect to the service’s design and/or architecture when addressed.
