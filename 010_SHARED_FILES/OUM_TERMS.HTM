A

Abstracted Identity
A digital personal devoid of any personally identifiable information.

Acceptance
The approval, typically by a customer or user, of a project work product.

Acceptance Test
A test typically used for end user acceptance and often performed manually. For more information, see the the section on Acceptance Testing in the OUM Testing Process Overview.

Access Control
1.  The ability to manage which users or groups of users may have the privilege to retrieve, create, update, or delete data held in a repository, such as a relational database. 2.  A concept in computer security used to enforce privilege separation.

Access Control List (ACL)
A concept used to enforce privilege separation. It is a means of determining the appropriate access rights to a given object depending on capability aspects reflected in tabular form.

Access Management
Facilitates authentication of network entities and authorization of their access to network resources.

Account Provisioning
The process of creating an account for a given application and managing the account's entitlements to allow and control its access to the resources managed by the application.

Accountability
The legitimacy of action enabled by the potential of negative feedback after a decision or action.

Activity
A set of tasks related either by topic, dependencies, data, common skills/roles, or work products. The tasks in an activity may be from different OUM processes. Activities in OUM begin and end in the same method phase. An activity is the next level of organization below a phase. Use this link for more information on activities in OUM.

Actor
An actor represents a role a user can play.  The same person could play different roles. An Actor can be one of four types; human, another system, a clock representing a time-based event, or a hardware device. Actors are represented as stick figures on UML Models. Actors with a hash mark are "business actors". Actors without a hash mark are "system actors". System Actors are those actors who would no longer be required, if the system were to be decommissioned. Business Actors are those who would continue to support the business, even if there were no system.

Actuals
Information gathered during a project concerning the actual amount of time, finances or resources expended on a task.

Ad Hoc Federation Pattern
A federation approach characterized by bilateral relationships between organizations wishing to create a federated identity arrangement.

Ad Hoc Level Maturity Model
The first level of system complexity descried by the Software Engineering Institute (SEI) at Carnegie Mellon University.

Agile Methods
There are several Methodologies in the Software Development realm that are referred to as "Agile Methods". These include Dynamic System Development Method (DSDM), Scrum, Extreme Programming (XP) and others. Agile methods have common characteristics, and both iterative and incremental. OUM can utilize Agile methodologies generally promote a project management process that encourages frequent inspection and adaptation, a leadership philosophy that encourages teamwork, self-organization and accountability, a set of engineering leading practices that allow for rapid delivery of high-quality software, and a business approach that aligns development with customer needs and company goals.13

Anonymized Data
Data devoid of any connection to an individual's identity.

Application
One or multiple computer programs that logically belong together and enable people to use a computer as a tool to accomplish specific related tasks. An example of this is a Commercial Off The Shelf (COTS) Human Resources application, containing programs to support Human Resources processes, such as employee maintenance, payroll, etc. An application may also be custom built. .

Application Architecture
1. The structure and behavior of applications used by a business, how the applications interact with other applications and with business users. Focused on the data consumed and produced by applications rather than the internal structure of the application. Applications in the IT Portfolio may be mapped to business functions and to application platform technologies. One of several Architecture domains that comprise Enterprise Architecture. 2. Application (or Component) architecture: The internal structure, the modularization of software, within an application. This software architecture at the lowest level of granularity, is usually below the level of modularization that solution architects define.13

Approach
A variation or subset of a method, packaged in order to efficiently support the delivery of a service.

Architectural Prototype
A prototype that has been developed to address architectural design decisions and test the integration of technical products and services. See also PROTOTYOPE.

Architectural View
A representation of the overall architecture that is meaningful to one or more stakeholders. A set of views is used to communicate to, and to be understood by all the stakeholders and enable them to verify that the system will address their concerns. An architect is interested in the architecture in its full scope and detail, but different stakeholders have different conceptions about the architecture and therefore require different views that focus on the concerns they deem relevant and leave out unnecessary information. As an example, in a building, one architectural view may be the electrical system.

Architecture-Centric
In the context of the software lifecycle, architecture-centric means that the system’s architecture is used as a primary artifact for conceptualizing, constructing, managing and evolving the system that is being implemented. Use this link for more information on the term Architecture-Centric as it applies to OUM.

Architecture Domain
A broad view of an enterprise or system one of the main pillars of enterprise architecture. It is a partial representation of a whole system that addresses several areas of concern for the enterprise stakeholders. Some examples of Architecture Domains are business, data, application and technical.

Artifact
A piece of information that is produced, modified or used by a process, defines an area of responsibility, and is subject to version control. An artifact an be a model, a model element or a document.

Assertion
A statement declared or stated positively about an individual's personal history.

Attributes Definition
In a user-centric system, the user controls the existence (or not) of the attribute related to the user, the authority over the value(s) of the attribute and control over the release of the attribute(s) to other parties, either by the subject or the authority.

Attributes of Identity
Data elements which distinguish an individual from another individual or group of individuals.

Auditability Authentication Systems
Operational transparency and documentation regarding the secure identification of system users without compromise or exposure of private authentication information, a legal mandate for regulatory compliance.

Authentication
1. The process of determining whether someone or something is, in fact, who or what it is declared to be. 2. The process by which an application or a security system ascertains the entitlements of a network entity or a user.

Authorization Decision Assertion (ADA)
A declaration that says whether a given subject has been granted specific permissions to access a particular resource.

Authorization Patterns
The aggregation of access provisioning methods and techniques.

Authorization Policies
Declarations that define entitlements of a security principal and any constraints related to that entitlement.

Boundary Class Diagram
The Boundary Class Diagram contains the design of the boundary classes.

Budget
A plan for determining in advance the expenditure of time, money, etc.

Build
An executable version of the system, usually for a specific part of the system. Development proceeds through a succession of builds.

Business Activity Monitoring (BAM)
By Deploying a SOA framework and using BAM for monitoring the services, business users can derive actionable timely intelligence on their business operations by monitoring the flow of data, service requests, and process invocations.  Oracle BAM pushes information to users via visual dashboards and alerts thereby helping improve effectiveness of operations and helps end users take informed decisions.

Business Architecture
The structure and activities within a business system. Stated from the business system perspective (not necessarily in regards to computer information systems). Describes business goals, functions, capabilities, processes, roles and events of the enterprise. Business functions and processes are often related to the applications and the data that they require. One of several Architecture domains that comprise Enterprise Architecture.

Business Object
A physical or logical object of significance to a business; for example, a sales order, department, assembly, item, balance, or invoice. A business object is analogous to a class in object-oriented terminology.

Business Process
1. A series of tasks designed to produce a product, service or major business deliverable. 2. A collection of inter-related tasks, which solve a particular issue.

Business Process and Use Case-Driven
Business processes and use cases are used as the primary artifacts for establishing the desired behavior of the system and for communicating this behavior among the stakeholders.

Business Process Execution Language (BPEL) PM
Oracle Business Process Execution Language (BPEL) Process Manager BPEL PM is a plug-and-play, standard-based infrastructure for integrating systems, services and people activities into easy-to-change process flows. It can be used to deliver both composite applications (web service orchestration, J2EE process flows) and data integration applications (file-to-DB, DB-to-DB, OracleApps-to-X)service methodology. BPEL provides Technology that orchestrates and automates the execution of business processes that involve human and system interactions during which documents, information or tasks are passed from one participant to another for action, according to a set of procedural rules. The BPEL Process Automation technology includes graphic design tools for mapping out a process definition (including its users, steps, rules, and conditions), and a process engine that executes the process.

Business Process Management
Refers to a set of activities which organizations can perform to either optimize their business processes or adapt them to new organizational needs.

Business Process Model (BPM)
A graphical representation of a business process. Models serve as a communication tool and verification device to ensure correctness of understanding and to document key business processes. Business Process Models are used as a requirements gathering tool.

Business Process Modeling
A technique that is used to understand the business processes of an organization and look at the business requirements that are satisfied by a particular business process flow.

Business Rule
1. Business perspective; A directive, intended to influence or guide business behavior, in support of business policy that has been formulated in response to an opportunity threat, strength or weakness. 2. IT perspective; a statement that defines or constrains an aspect of the business. It is intended to assert business structure or control or influence the behavior of the business.

Capability Based Access Control
The principle of designing user programs such that they directly share capabilities with each other according to the principle of least privilege, and the operating system infrastructure necessary to make such transactions efficient and secure.

Capability Management
Defined as a set of interlinking business activities or functions aimed at achieving a balance between the sustainable use of a discretionary business function.

Centralized Assertion Services
Services that generate identity assertions as part of the identity management infrastructure. OracleAS Single Sign-On is an example of an assertion service that generates identity assertions. OracleAS Certificate Authority (OCA) is another type of assertion service because the X.509v3 certificates that it generates are indeed assertions about a network entity's identity and its entitlements.

Challenge-Response System
A program that replies to a message from an unknown sender by subjecting the sender to a test designed to differentiate humans from automated senders.

Change
A deviation from a currently established baseline.

Change Control
see ISSUE RESOLUTION MANAGEMENT.

Change Request
1.  A request for a change to the required behavior of a system, usually from a user as a result of reviewing current behavior.  2.  The mechanism by which a change is requested, investigated, resolved and approved; see also IMPACT ANALYSIS.

Class
A description of a set of objects that share the same responsibilities, relationships, operations, attributes and semantics.

Cloud Computing
A term used to define computing that occurs remotely in a location accessible via the Internet. The owner of the computing resources usually offers access based on some form of lease terms. Software as a Service (SaaS) is one form of Cloud Computing. Others include Infrastructure as a Service (IaaS) and Platform as a Service (PaaS). Each provides a means to lease computing capabilities as opposed to owning them. Cloud computing is a model for enabling convenient, on-demand network access to a shared pool of configurable computing resources (for example, networks, servers, storage, applications, and services) that can be rapidly provisioned and released with minimal management effort or service provider interaction. Characteristics of both Public and Private Clouds that differentiate cloud from traditional “hosting” services include high efficiency, high availability and elastic capacity. For more information, go to the Supplemental Guidance page; see also HYBRID CLOUD, IaaS, PaaS, PRIVATE CLOUD, PUBLIC CLOUD and SaaS.

Commercial Off the Shelf (COTS)
A term used to describe hardware or software that is ready-made and available for sale, lease or license to the general public.  Often used as an alternative to custom or one-off development. Oracle's licensed software products would be an example of COTS software.

Completion Criteria
Standards or rules which determine completion of a task to an acceptable level of quality.

Component
1.  A part or section of a work product. A component may be the output of a task step or task.  2.  The physical packaging of model elements, such as, design classes created in the Design process. Components have a trace relationship to the model elements they implement. Some standard types of components include executables, files containing source code, library, etc.

Conceptual Prototype
Also known as a mockup-prototype, an abstract prototype or a paper prototype. It is comprised of sketches of screens via which the user will interact with the system and the execution flow of such screens. For programs that will not be implemented through screens, the prototype only shows the execution flow. This prototype may consist of presentation slides, flip chart sheets or whiteboards illustrating how the layout will work. It should also contain flow diagrams for screens (also known as navigation diagrams), highlighting the main navigational methods necessary for the system. Activity diagrams may also be used to demonstrate the flow, see also FUNCTIONAL PROTOTYPE, PROTOTYOPE.

Concern
An interest/need held by a stakeholder regarding the initiative in which they are involved. Some of these concerns can be common with other stakeholders. In effect, a concern is an area or topic of interest.
Stakeholder concerns can be captured for an individual role or a group of stakeholders, for example, the enterprise architect and the Enterprise Architecture Authority Team may share some of the same concerns.

Configuration
A named set of configuration items.  Configurations are used to hierarchically organize configuration items in order to facilitate their management; see also CONFIGURATION ITEM.

Configuration Change
The implementation of one or more change requests which leaves the configuration in an internally consistent state; see also IMPACT ANALYSIS.

Configuration Item
A work product or component which is placed under configuration management.

Configuration Management
The process of managing hardware, software, data, and any other documentation needed during the development, testing, and implementation of information systems.

Consolidated Work Product
A work product that consolidates, indexes or packages several work products for the convenience of downstream consumption or review. OUM provides several work products that are designed to be used for this purpose. For example, the Requirements Specification (RD.140) may be used to consolidate or index several requirements work products. Consolidated work products may be considered as candidate deliverables. See WORK PRODUCT, KEY WORK PRODUCT, DELIVERABLE.

Consulting Grade Level
A grade level assigned to Oracle Services consulting  resources used to calculate the cost of a resource’s labor; 1-Administrative Assistant to 10-Regional Vice President.

Contingency
Work effort allotted in a work plan for all unforeseen but possible occurrences of additional work.

Contribution Margin
See MARGIN AMOUNT and MARGIN PERCENTAGE.

Control Class Diagram
The Control Class Diagram contains the design of the control classes. J2EE design patterns can be used to model and design such a classes. Depending of the technology used, the design may be different in order to accommodate restrictions related to the target technology. 

Controlled Document
A document which constitutes or represents a project work product for approval internally or by the customer, and is subject to change control.

Cost
The amount allotted or spent to acquire, deliver or  produce anything, for example, the cost of labor to deliver consulting services, the amount spent on incidental costs to deliver consulting services, the amount spent on hardware, software, etc.

A term used to describe a component of technical architecture that provides a conduit for data to be shared across disparate systems.  This term refers specifically to data that is associated with a customer.  Often used for customer data integration, a Customer Data Hub facilitates identity management across the enterprise.

Domain13
1. In problem solving and software engineering a "Domain" model can be thought of as a conceptual model of a system which describes the various entities involved in that system and their relationships. In Database Theory, a data domain is a set of all permitted values. In data management and database analysis, a data domain refers to all the unique values which a data element may contain. The rule for determining the domain boundary may be as simple as a data type with enumerated list of values.

2. A problem domain is the area of expertise or application that needs to be examined to solve a problem. A problem domain is examining only the topics you are interested in, and excluding all others. For example, if you were developing a system about clothing, you wouldn't include electronics in your problem domain. In this example the domain refers to topics solely within your interest: clothing.

Enterprise Service Bus (ESB)
ESB refers to a software architecture component, of middleware infrastructure products, based on web services standards.  It provides foundational services for SOA via and event driven and XML-based messaging engine (the bus).  Allows integration architects to exploit the value of messaging without writing code.  Oracle ESB is a standards based infrastructure component of the Oracle SOA Suite. Oracle ESB features a multi-protocol message bus with centralized monitoring management of distributed services where all the services are exposed as standard web services using WSDL (Web Services Design Language).  Oracle ESB contains message flows utilizing adapters, transformations and routing rules to distribute data throughout and beyond the enterprise.  The Oracle Enterprise Service Bus system is made up of the following service components designer, repository and registry, runtime server and management and monitoring console.

I

Identity
the set of attributes that uniquely identify a network entity. A network entity can be an application user or a service in the enterprise network. Usually a network entity can have different accounts that it uses to access various applications in the network. The applications identify the different accounts by using different attributes of the network entity. For example, a user can be known in the e-mail service by his or her e-mail ID, whereas that same user can be known in the human resources application by his or her employee number. The global set of such attributes constitutes the identity of the entity.

Identity Administration
The act of managing information associated with the identity of a network entity and their associated attributes. The information can be used by the identity management infrastructure itself to determine administrative privileges.

Identity Abstracted
The set of behavioral or personal characteristics by which an individual is recognizable as a member of a group.

Identity and Credential Management
Identity and Credential Management establishes and manages the identity of the user throughout the lifecycle of that identity from the initial provisioning of the user's identity, the defining of access rights and following a logical workflow to retirement of the identity.

Identity Attribute
A property of a digital subject that may have zero or more values and is used by a digital identity system.

Identity Data Audit
An operational transparency technique which involves the asking of additional questions about the data in the inventory.

Identity Database
A special database that stores and manages identity information across an enterprise.

Identity Management (IdM)
The management of a user's credentials and how the might access an online system. The concept of identity management in the physical world is strongly linked to context and relationships. When computers and the services they support seek to interoperate, the incorporation of identities becomes important. The infrastructure components that constitute identity management include a data store (usually a directory or relational database) and processes that read and update the data, such as authentication and authorization that provide access control to a resource. A directory such as LDAP is a significant component in identity management but is not the last stop. The directory can provide a central place to store credentials but still allow authentication and authorization policies to be embedded in an application. The ideal scenario is to externalize these rules so that they can be executed anywhere, including in an application or by a centralized security service.

Identity Management Policies
Policies that govern the management of identities in an enterprise. These include naming policies and security policies.

Identity Management Realms
Collections of identities and associated policies that are typically used when enterprises want to isolate user populations and enforce different identity management policies for each population. The various identity management realms that are created are not hierarchical but are at the same level.

Identity Mapping
A server dependent architecture which enables an enterprise to manage the multiple user registries and identities that in turn enable users to access multiple applications with a single sign-on.

Identity Policy Assertion Services
Processes that generate verifiable tokens or assertions about the identity of an entity or its authorizations. Network entities present these assertions to other services that the entities access.

Identity Provisioning
The act of establishing the identity of a network entity and the necessary credentials to facilitate authentication of the identity.

Impact Analysis
The process of understanding the complete effect of a particular change; see also CHANGE REQUEST.

Increment
1. An increment is the difference between the release of one iteration and the release of the next iteration. Use this link for a more information on increments in OUM.  2. A small and manageable part of the system, usually the delta or difference between two successive builds. Each iteration results in at least one (new) build and thus adds an increment to the system. However, a sequence of builds may be created within an iteration, each one adding a small increment to the system. Thus an iteration adds a larger increment to the system, possibly accumulated over several builds.

Incremental
Creating something one piece at a time and integrating the pieces into the whole a little at a time.

Information Architecture
Consists of a model that describes how the business sees information and its data structures, formulated in terms that are understandable for the business. The information architecture is technology and application agnostic, and consists of an Enterprise Domain Model that describes the entities, their relationships and key attributes and a description of the information sources.

Information Systems (IS)
A system for managing and processing information, usually computer-based.  Also, a functional group within a business that manages the development and operations of the business’ information systems.

Infrastructure as a Service (IaaS)
The capability provided to the consumer is to provision processing, storage, networks, and other fundamental computing resources where the consumer is able to deploy and run arbitrary software that can include operating systems and applications. The consumer does not manage or control the underlying cloud infrastructure but has control over operating systems, storage, deployed applications, and possibly limited control of select networking components (for example, host firewalls). Includes Infrastructure; VM, OS Servers and Storage as a Service; see also CLOUD COMPUTING, HYBRID CLOUD, IaaS, PaaS, PRIVATE CLOUD, PUBLIC CLOUD and SaaS.

Initial Operational Capability Milestone
The Initial Operational Capability Milestone is the third key project milestone in a OUM project produced at the end of the Construction Phase.  At this point, a decision is made on whether the application, infrastructure and users are ready to move to operation.  The Initial Operation Capability Milestone is also considered a "beta" release.

Iteration
1. An iteration is a distinct set of activities conducted according to a devoted (iteration) plan and evaluation criteria that results in a release, either internal or external. Use this link for a more information on iterations in OUM.  2. Indicates the number of times or degree to which a task or task group should be repeated, in order to either increase the quality of the task/group work products to a desired level, to add sufficient level of detail, or to refine and expand them on the basis of user feedback.  A task or group may be "singly iterated" (the expectation is that it will be performed without repetition in the project), or "multiply iterated" (it will be performed successively, multiple times), resulting in a single work product for each task (note this is not true for multiply instantiated tasks).  For multiple iterations, it is important for planning purposes to give notes on the number of times or the degree to which a task or group should be iterated, based on experience; see also ITERATIVE BUILD, ITERATIVE DEVELOPMENT, and INCREMENT. The OUM definition of an iteration is consistent with UML, but may be slightly different from the iterations in a CDM FT or DSDM-like approach.  With OUM, an iteration results in an increment.  "Iterations", as defined by  CDM FT or DSDM, can also be used in OUM.  In this sense, a task or group of tasks is repeated to add additional detail.  In general, when the term "Iteration" is used in OUM, it is referring to the former definition that results in an increment.

Iteration planning involves assessment of project risks, agreement on scope, iteration evaluation criteria, iteration approach, work allocation and when the assessment of the iteration will take place. There are some excellent books on managing iterative projects6 that you may want to review.

A description of an iteration that integrates several elements: workers, the activities and tasks they perform, the work products they produce. Redoing something several times, increasing its richness, comprehensiveness, and consistency each time.

MoSCoW List
A prioritized list of requirements by the business (both functional and supplemental). See Task RD.045 Prioritize Requirements for more information on how MoSCoW is used in OUM. MoSCoW provides a method for focusing on the relative importance of requirements. The acronym MoSCoW stands for:

Must Have - critical requirements without which the system cannot function. They are fundamental to the system. The requirements define the minimum usable subset and are within the scope of the contract.

Should Have - requirements that are important but not critical to the system. In a less time-constrained development, these requirements would be mandatory. The Should Have requirements can be sacrificed if development of Must Haves or Should Haves takes more effort than estimated. In most contracts, this is the lowest category level of in-scope requirements.

Could Have - requirements that are desirable but which could be left out of the increment under development. More Could Have requirements can be delivered if development of Must Haves and Should Haves takes less effort than estimated. In most contracts, this is the first category of out-of-scope requirements.

Won't Have - requirements that are valuable but which will not be delivered by the increment under development. These requirements are out-of-scope.

In many other contexts, a broader term is used, and covers both the application and the system as defined in the previous statements.

System Production Milestone
The System Production Milestone is produced at the end of the Transition phase.  At this point, the stakeholders decide if the goals and objectives set for the project have been met and if a new increment should begin.

System Reference Architecture (SRA)
A diagramming and documentation process that involves participating system vendors and their partners physically building and documenting actual instances of current and planned infrastructures.

System under Discussion (SuD)
The system that is the main topic of the analysis, or design work being described in the work products being produced by a project. The abbreviation SuD may be referred to as the System under Development.

T

Task
A unit of work that is done in delivering a service.  A task is the smallest trackable item on a project workplan, and forms the basis for a work breakdown structure.  The minimum elapsed time for a task iteration or instantiation should be one day.  The maximum should be two weeks; see also WORK BREAKDOWN STRUCTURE.

Task Dependency
The relationship between two tasks where the start or end date of the successor task is constrained by the start or end date of the predecessor task.

Task Dependency Network
A network of tasks and task dependencies where each node is a task and each link is a task dependency; see also CRITICAL PATH METHOD (CPM) NETWORK.

Task Iteration
Indicates the number of times or degree to which a task or activity (task group) should be repeated in a project plan, in order to either increase the quality of the task/group work products to a desired level, to add sufficient level of detail, or to refine and expand them on the basis of user feedback. A task or activity (task group) may be “singly iterated” (SI) (the expectation is that it will be performed without repetition in the project), or “multiply iterated” (MI) (it will be performed successively, multiple times), resulting in a single work product for each task.

Task Step
A discreet step to be done in executing a task.

Technology Architecture
May be used interchangeably with infrastructure architecture. The structure and behavior of the technology infrastructure within an Enterprise. Covers the hardware configuration, infrastructure applications or system software that run on them, the infrastructure services they offer to business applications as well as the protocols and networks which connect the applications. One of several Architecture domains that comprise Enterprise Architecture.

Technical Architecture
OUM process with the goal of designing an information systems architecture to support and realize the business vision.

Technique
A specific approach to performing a task.  A methodical means of handling and communicating complex details.

Technique Guidance
A set of steps and an approach that can be conditionally applied to the completion of a task or multiple tasks. Examples are meetings or workshops — depending on the size of the project, one or the other may be chosen to complete the work of a task(s).

Technology Guidance
A set of guidelines that apply to a specific type of technology. This guidance may be applied when the specified technology is being used to complete the system under discussion (SuD). Examples are Service-Oriented Architecture (SOA) and Clouding Computing.

Template
A tool designed to aid in production of a work product; a template that gives the format and structure of a work product; see also WORK PRODUCT.

Test Case
A set of conditions or variables under which a tester will determine whether an application or software system is working correctly or not. A test case could be a step in a test scenario, or a part of a test suite. Use this link for a more information on testing in OUM.

Test Criteria
Test criteria describe the conditions under which a test has passed or failed. Use this link for a more information on testing in OUM.

Test Item
Something that should be tested. Use this link for a more information on testing in OUM.

Test Plan
A plan that is produced iteratively along with the development iterations to ensure that the plan reflects what is actually developed in the iterations. The test plan details the approach described in the Testing Strategy, and includes information about tester roles and responsibilities, test types, test data and test estimates and scheduling. Use this link for a more information on testing in OUM.

Test Scenario
A test often formed as a scenario, or hypothetical story. Use this link for a more information on testing in OUM.

Test Script
A collection of test cases that should be used to test a software program to show that it has some specified set of behaviors. These steps are often executed automatically, but can just as well be executed manually. Use this link for a more information on testing in OUM.

Test Sequence
The order in which something is tested. Use this link for a more information on testing in OUM.

Test Suite
A test suite is a collection of test cases that should be used to test a software program to show that it has some specified set of behaviors. Use this link for a more information on testing in OUM.

Test Type
Any possible kind of test to which a test item can be subjected (functional and non-functional). Use this link for a more information on testing in OUM.

Testing Approach
Describes the method that should be used for testing. Use this link for a more information on testing in OUM.

Testing Strategy
Describes the actual approach that should be used for the different kind of tests that should be performed as described in the Testing Requirements. It describes the project direction as it relates to testing. It includes how testing should be performed in the various stages of the project, which tools should be used (if any), how and what metrics should be collected, how test data should be produced and what testing environments should be used in the tests. The strategy also defines the acceptance criteria of the solution. Use this link for a more information on testing in OUM.

Ticket-Based Management
A system which supports the authentication of users, services and service providers over different domains where in tokens (tickets) are used to verify correctness of the requested service.

Timebox
A project management technique that a fixes the duration and resources of a task, or set of tasks, and forces the scope of the project to be adjusted based on the time available to complete the task(s).  The contingency for under-estimation of the work is provided by a prioritized list of features left out if necessary.  The contingency for over-estimation is provided by a prioritized list of features that should be added in if time allows.

TOGAF14
The Open Group Architecture Framework. An Architectural Framework – i.e. a set of methods and tools for developing a broad range of different IT Architectures. It enables IT users to design, evaluate, and build the right architecture for their organization, and reduces the costs of planning, designing, and implementing architectures based on open systems solutions. This framework may be utilized during an OUM Envision Engagement. See the TOGAF Overview for more information. Also see EADF Oracle's Enterprise Architecture Development Framework.

Tool
Software applications, templates, or any other utility suggested to facilitate the completion of a particular task.

Tool Guidance
A set of steps and an approach that can be conditionally applied to the completion of a task or multiple tasks using the specified tool It is only applicable when the specified tool (subject of the guidance) is being utilized by the project. Examples are JDeveloper and ADF.

Top-Down Estimate
A high-level work effort estimate.  This type of estimate is derived by taking a total project estimate and dividing it among the project’s phases, activities, and tasks.

Uncontrolled Document
A document which is produced once for information only, and is not subject to formal approval or change control.

Unified Process (UP)
UP is the Unified Process.  UP is a software development process.  OUM is based on UP with Oracle Proprietary Method concepts and processes applied.

UML (Unified Modeling Language)
UML is a standard language for specifying, visualizing, constructing and documenting the components of a software system.  It can also be used for business modeling and to model non-software systems.  UML is a modeling convention which brings standard modeling practices together in one set of conventions for syntax of models.

Unit Test
A test of a distinct unit of functionality. A unit of functionality is not directly dependent on the completion of another unit of functionality. Unit tests are the finest level of granularity in testing. Use this link for a more information on testing in OUM.

Use Case
The specification of a set of actions performed by a system, which yield an observable result, that is, typically, of value for one or more actors or other stakeholders of the system (Source: UML: Superstructure v2.1, OMG Group). Use this link to see more about how use cases evolve in OUM.

Use Case Realization
A use case is "realized" in the Design process. The work effort of moving from the Analysis Model (artifacts such as the activity diagram, sequence diagram, collaboration diagram etc.), into the Design Model is often described as "use case realization". Use case realization happens in order to delineate the concerns of the system specifications as described in the use cases, from the concerns of the designers of the system. Use case realization involves organizing the use case and any information related to it, which belong in the design model. During use case realization, the Analysis Model is enriched adding the level of design required to build or specify the components.

User
A person who uses a system to perform a business function.

User Centric
A user has credentials which may be self- or IDP-generated and they can use those to make trusted assertions to third parties even if the latter have no prior trust relationship with the user or the user's IDP.

User-Centric End Point Identity Access Management System
A solution that balances the enterprise and IT requirements for monitoring, control and compliance with the end-user imperative of convenience.

User-Centric Identity
The methods used to create, maintain, disburse, distribute, modify and remove any data of an identity nature wherein the subject (user, person) is the primary maintainer of all his/her digital personae, their attributes and the relationships between them.

User-Centric Paradigm
An identity management philosophy which dictates that the user should be in control of their own identity attributes and that there should be no central repository of their personal data controlled by a third party.

User-Centric System
Defined as a system that brings together multiple components of a user's world into a central focus.

User Interface Standards Prototype
Used to explore the “look and feel” of a system. It may contain example page layouts, generic screens, navigation mechanisms, and data query styles. Even when standard products are used, this prototype can help define the specific graphic elements and branding that will be inserted into an applications environment. Where several products are being used or, where there is a high level of flexibility in the user interface functionality, this prototype promotes a consistent look and feel across an enterprise; see also PROTOTYPE.

Utilization
The amount of time a staff member books to a project accounting system; see also BILLABLE UTILIZATION and NON-BILLABLE UTILIZATION.

Value-added Chain Diagrams (VACD)

Value Added Chain Diagrams visualize on a high-level the business functions that add value to a business, and the sequence in which they generally proceed.  They visualize the value added chain, and it is drawn without any events or rules. It is normally drawn as arrowed boxes following each other, where each arrowed box represents a business function. Also see Functional or Process Modeling Technique.

View
1.  An initial tailoring of the workplan specific to a focus area, discipline, service offering or technology/product architecture.  2.  A means of accessing a subset of data in a database.  see also ARCHITECTURAL VIEW.

Viewpoint
A means for specifying an architectural view that prescribes how views address particular concerns of the stakeholders and how models are constructed. A view may have many different representations depending on the purpose and the target stakeholder. A CIO will require a different representation and abstraction level as compared to a service designer. A viewpoint establishes the conventions by which a view is created, depicted, and analyzed. In this way, a view conforms to a viewpoint. The viewpoint determines the languages (including notations, model, and language) to be used to describe the view, and any associated modeling methods or analysis techniques to be applied to these representations of the view. These languages and techniques are used to yield results relevant to the concerns addressed by the viewpoint. For example, if you have the electrical system for a building as a view, one viewpoint (end user) may be a drawing displaying the power outlets and switches, while another viewpoint (electrical engineer) for the same view may a drawing of the complete wiring.
