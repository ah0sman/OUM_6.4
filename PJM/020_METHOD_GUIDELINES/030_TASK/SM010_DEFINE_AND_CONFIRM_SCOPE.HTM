SM.010 - DEFINE AND CONFIRM SCOPE

The purpose of this task is to document and confirm the characteristics and boundaries of the Scope Baseline for the project. The Scope Baseline is the scope definition for the project.

DETAILED DESCRIPTION

If the scope of your project is clearly defined in sufficient enough detail by a contract, this task may not be necessary and it may be acceptable to refer to the contract for the baseline scope. However, if the project is not covered by a contract (for example, an internal project) or there is not sufficient enough detail in the contract, it is very important to capture the Scope Baseline.

The validated Scope Baseline enables the project team to perform more detailed planning and guides the project team’s work in further detailing the requirements in Project Execution and Control. The Scope Baseline is static and provides a basis for evaluation and comparison for the formal change management process.

WORK PRODUCT

Validated Scope

The Validated Scope is documented in the Scope Baseline component of the Project Management Plan (PMP). The Scope Baseline for the project provides a concise summary of the project objectives, scope, out of scope, the product(s) to be delivered, major activities, major work products, and major milestones, It provides a common understanding of the project’s scope among stakeholders.

APPROACH

Once again, if your project is covered by a contract, it may be acceptable and even preferable to just refer to the contract. By referring to the contract for the Scope Baseline, you avoid creating confusion and possibly even making an error.

If your project is not covered by a contract, refer to the following sections to document the Validated Scope (Scope Baseline)

Description

Provide a clear, concise summary description of the scope of the project.

Add details appropriate to the project. For example, add a list/table of applications, products, modules, business processes, business flows, customizations, integrations or interfaces, etc. Include what makes sense for your project using the terminology appropriate for your project.

Exclusions

Document any known out-of-scope items.

Constraints

Document and constraints. Constraints are conditions that limit the project team’s choices for completing the project.

Examples of constraints are:

  • Project budget
  • Release dates
  • Resource availability
  • Holiday schedules

Pay close attention to customer-related constraints. It is imperative to uncover customer plans for expansion, acquisitions, or other competing projects that might impact the current project.

Assumptions

Document and assumptions. Assumptions are those beliefs around which decisions involving the project scope were made. For planning purposes, assumptions should be considered as “real and certain.” Assumptions may impact the project’s scope, schedule, or cost if they turn out to be invalid.

Examples of assumptions are:

  • Modules or business flows will be implemented as delivered without modification
  • Customer project team members will have ready access to all corporate policy and procedure documentation

All assumptions concerning scope, resources, user participation, issues resolution, QA and similar items should be documented.
