TIPS FOR PROJECT MANAGERS USING OUM FOR THE FIRST TIME

So, you are a project manager for a development project using Oracle Unified Method (OUM). If you are like most project managers, there are many competing priorities for your time and your attention, especially during Project Start Up.

OUM is based on the Unified Process and software development leading practices, especially for Oracle technology. It brings benefits to many stakeholders but most of all, it benefits you, the project manager. The OUM approach gives you better control, reduces risk and anticipates problems. But this depends on your use of the method. It is a tool to leverage your experience and capacity, not to substitute the need for planning and careful attention to project execution.

This tip sheet is intended to give you some key links into the OUM content. This is most valuable in furthering your understanding of where to find guidelines in OUM that are especially helpful to a project manager using OUM for the first few times.

OUM MANTRA

If you attend OUM classes or read OUM training material, you already know that OUM is:

  • Iterative and Incremental
  • Use Case and Business Process-Driven
  • Architecture-Centric

It is possible to get all the OUM work product templates and have a standard plan driven "waterfall" or cascade type project. You would lose most of the benefits of the OUM approach. You should understand these three points before beginning your project.

NOTE: It is also important to install the latest version of the Oracle Unified Method Pack before beginning your next project to make sure you have the most current material available.

WHAT ITERATIVE AND INCREMENTAL MEAN TO THE PROJECT MANAGER

You probably saw the OUM diagram with waves representing the processes and the five phases: Inception, Elaboration, Construction, Transition, and Production. You noticed some familiar processes: Analysis, Design, Implementation, Testing... all this is known territory if you understand the basics of a methodology.

Unfortunately, you may not notice some of the most important elements: the iterations and milestones down below. They drive your entire project.

Let's first understand iterations. In OUM (or any other iterative approach), you divide the project time in periods of time, usually from 2 to 6 weeks (some prefer 2~4 weeks), called iterations. During each of these periods, the team executes tasks in order to meet the iteration's goal(s). Therefore, the term, "iterative" means that work on an OUM project is divided into a series of "iterations" that are essentially run as mini-projects.

At the end of the iteration, the overall effectiveness of the iteration is evaluated and changes are made to improve the process in following iterations. Preferably, iterations should be timeboxed, that is, they should have a predetermined duration. The "scope" of each iteration should be understood by the team and requirements should be prioritized (we'll talk about MoSCoW in the next section).

In each iteration, the team executes a little of all the processes (look again at the OUM diagram – each vertical slice has a piece of everything). Let's try one example: imagine you have first iteration of Elaboration (timeboxed for ending in 3 weeks). During this period, the following activities/tasks are executed:

  • Use cases in the first iteration group are detailed.
  • An initial Functional Prototype is developed and presented to ambassador users.
  • Application setups are created and documented.
  • Requirements are mapped and gap resolutions are examined.

Additional tasks may be executed for the iteration depending on the scope of the project and the identified risks. As the project manager, you are responsible for preparing and maintaining the Implementation Plan that spans the entire project, as well as creating and updating the Iteration Plan for each iteration.

Incremental means that the system is developed in chunks, iteration by iteration. Each iteration results in an increment, which is a release of the system that contains added or improved functionality compared with the previous release. At the end of an iteration, the resulting increment of functionality is presented to users and requirements are re-evaluated so as to plan the next iteration.

Putting this all together, iterative and incremental in OUM means that the system is developed through a series of mini-projects (iterative) and in smaller portions at a time (incremental), allowing the project team to take advantage of what was learned during development of earlier parts or versions of the system and incorporate external feedback from project stakeholders.

For more information, refer to Planning a Project using the Oracle Unified Method (OUM) . For further information on managing iterative projects, see Managing Iterative Software Development Projects.

WHAT MOSCOW MEANS TO THE PROJECT MANAGER

The MoSCoW List is probably the most important tool in managing an OUM project. The manager should be responsible for providing the team values, focus and priorities. The MoSCoW List should be used to reach these objectives.

Simply stated, the MoSCoW List registers the known business requirements. Each requirement is classified by the first letter of: Must, Should, Could or Wo'’t.

You should make it clear to all stakeholders that Ms and Ss are the scope for this iteration and Cs and Ws are registered as possible future functionality.

There are a lot of points to discuss about this "simple" list.

  • You may want to use these expanded definitions in addition to the guidance in the Prioritize Requirements (RD.045) task :
    • M - What is absolutely essential for the current iteration
    • S - What is planned to be ready for the current iteration but we have a strategy to operate some time without it (or at least until the next iteration)
    • C - What is left out of the scope of the current iteration, but can be included if we have more time or if some "Shoulds" fall out and are not should anymore
    • W - What is left out of the scope for now and probably for the next few iterations as well
  • Some PMPs would argue that including Cs on the increment scope would be a "gold-plating." Note that changes on the MoSCoW List should be considered change requests and go through your Integrated Change Control Process. Don’t plan to deliver your Cs – but be aware that requirements can change as a project progresses.
  • It is important for the project manager show the project stakeholders that not everything is top priority. Try to keep the iteration scope requirements classified 50% as M and 50% as S. That gives the team the focus on what is more important for the business – as well as, can explain to the stakeholders that there are some technical priorities that should be considered.
  • The prioritization should consider the business value of the requirement, as well as the technical risks for the whole solution.
  • Avoid using M++ or S-. This keeps the classifications clear to everyone.
  • After there is agreement on a current iteration's MoSCoW List, create another prioritization for your iteration. You may be implementing three use cases classified as M, but, as this is a timebox, you can further prioritize the three use case as one M, one S and one C.

HOW THE PROJECT MANAGER EVOLVES THE MOSCOW LIST

You should refine and evolve the MoSCoW List during the entire project.

  1. Start with a WBS (work breakdown structure) of all work to be done in a general view. Do some mind mapping and some brainstorming with post-its. Examining the list of OUM tasks is VERY useful. Some brainstorming and additional talk about bid transition material is also valuable.
  2. For the requirements, when using UML modeling, start with an actor-goal list (as Cockburn suggests in his book, Writing Effective Use Cases) – only the name of the use cases is required. Also include some supplemental requirements (aka non-functional requirements) that would require some work, not included in the WBS. Begin by calling this list the MoSCoW List – still incomplete.
  3. Obtain agreement with the stakeholders (requirements team, project sponsor, etc.) on the priorities – MSCWs. Sometimes this step should be anticipated before the scope is frozen for the iteration.
  4. You should then order the requirements by importance, Ms first, then Ss. We start to "ignore" Cs and Ws unless a new idea occurs.
    • At any step, new ideas for requirements are registered as Cs. If they are REALLY important then you should create a change request and meet with the Change Control Board. Otherwise, there must be an agreement that at the end of any iteration, all the Cs and Ws are re-evaluated for the next iteration.
  5. You may decide to use the MoSCoW List MS Excel work product to control the iterations. On the Use Case sheet, include the iteration number for that use case, who is responsible, and some comments. Sometimes you may include a column for planned and actual effort.
    • In the initial iterations (especially during Elaboration), there must be fewer use cases and more slippage. This is to be is expected. You should keep to the timeboxes. If you don’t reach the phase/milestone objectives, then plan to include a new iteration on that phase.
  6. Insert this data on the MS-Project plan and include some milestones for the iterations and also merge with the other tasks not related to the MoSCoW but on the WBS.

PHASES AND OUM MILESTONES

This is a very important topic for project managers.

In the OUM material you will find good descriptions of what’s expected in each phase and for reaching each milestone. It is important that you read them.

In an iterative project approach, it is easy to lose focus on the task objectives. The team mind set changes from phase to phase because the objectives of each phase are distinct. As a project manager, you are responsible for planning and providing focus for your team to satisfy the phase objectives. The key is to focus on reaching the milestones (LO, LA, IOC, SP and SO).

Read the descriptions provided in the Phase Overviews in OUM. Below are links to them along with an brief introduction on the meaning of these phases and milestones.

Inception: When you first begin on a new project you have an “initial date” mind set. You begin to identify the stakeholders. What are their real, perhaps not stated, objectives. You make an assessment of the information made available to the project team. Is the project feasible or not? Are the chosen technologies really adequate or does your initial assessment bring out some items which need further explanation? At the end of this initial period the team must know the main objectives and that there is a viable solution for this project. You leave the Inception phase and move on to Elaboration when you have achieved the Lifecycle Objective Milestone LO.

Elaboration: As stated earlier, each iteration has some parts of all the processes. For example, in a custom development project, the team starts coding in the first iteration. Of course, effort for the Requirements Analysis, Analysis and Design will be much greater. Nonetheless, in a custom development, it is a good idea to choose one important (architecturally-significant) use case and design, code and show it to the users. You may hear a lot of change requests and complaints about some parts of the process not complete. However, doing this puts working software in front of the stakeholders, often prompting more specific requirements.

This is intentional. While you're creating all the "normal" specification documents, the user of what you are preparing won’t "have a picture of" what they are approving or describing. If you show them some ready code or early prototypes, new ideas appear, some risks become problems and some "always do this" items become "in most cases." This is intentional because it is cheaper to treat these problems at early stages. You must be sure your ambassador users understand what is being created, or how the application will function. Risks are anticipated and managed or eliminated early on!

At the end of Elaboration you must:

  • Understand the whole picture of what is about to be constructed (requirements, with a better ambassador user understanding).
  • Know how difficult this technology is (you have already experimented some development).
  • Attack some of your worst architectural risks.
  • Create the skeleton that will support the whole development.

You exit Elaboration and move into Construction when you have achieved the Lifecycle Architecture Milestone LA.

In Construction the team has one big mission: build quality code efficiently and/or provide appropriate setups and customizations to an existing application (either custom developed or commercial-off-the-shelf). There can be some remaining decisions to make but this phase should use more activity oriented. Most major decisions should have been made in Elaboration.

Users start testing during Elaboration and continue to test during Construction.

Construction ends and Transition begins when you have achieved the Initial Operational Capability (IOC) milestone. For a custom development project, this would be when you have a Beta version of your whole system. For an Applications Implementation project, this would be when you have a version of the application up and running with accurate data.

Transition provides the steps to make Beta version (fully system and integration tested) ready to move to Production version and to have infrastructure and teams prepared for Go-Live, which is the System in Production milestone SP.

After that, you are in Production and there are some tasks specific for this phase. In a general, verify if everything is OK, treat problems, obtain acceptance and discuss the final MoSCoW List with organization. This is where you discuss the next project that will be done. At the end of Production you have reached the Sign Off Milestone SO.
