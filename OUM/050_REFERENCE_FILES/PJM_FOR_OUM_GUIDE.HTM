PROJECT MANAGEMENT IN OUM

MANAGING AN ORACLE UNIFIED METHOD (OUM) PROJECT

Oracle Unified Method (OUM) uses the Manage focus area [formerly the Project Management Method (PJM)] to provide a framework in which projects can be planned, estimated, controlled, and tracked in a consistent manner. This consistency is required in today's business environment, where projects often implement packages, develop application extensions, and create a data warehouse in order to satisfy a business need.

Project Management enables the the project manager to manage delivery of an agreed upon level of solution quality while planning for and controlling the scope, cost and schedule. 

If you have never managed a project with a Unified Process approach, or are not familiar with the terms "iterative" or "incremental, read the Tips for Project Managers and Planning a Project using the Oracle Unified Method (OUM). For further information on managing iterative projects, see Managing Iterative Software Development Projects.

Oracle Unified Method Implement Focus Area and OUM Manage Focus Area

Sometimes an organization prefers to use its own supporting methods with the Oracle Unified Method. OUM is adaptable and can be used in conjunction with a user's own supporting methods.

Most methods share certain common characteristics. For example, most methods break down a project into phases, processes, and tasks. From this perspective, it is possible to analyze the two methods and align (map) the similarities and differences at the phase, process, and task levels - this is referred to as method interlock.

OUM is intended as an implementation method and it assumes that the OUM Manage focus area or another project management methodology is in place. The OUM Manage focus area provides a number of key processes that are critical for a successful implementation, including:

  • Work Management
  • Risk Management
  • Issue and Problem Management
  • Communication Management
  • Quality Management
  • Configuration Management
  • Infrastructure Management

In cases where Oracle Consulting is not providing project management or the OUM Manage focus area is not in use, the requirement for these critical activities is not eliminated. If an alternative project management method is in place, the method may define

  • how work tasks are planned, assigned and tracked;
  • how risks, issues and problems are identified and tracked;
  • what types of regular communications such as status reporting are required and who should participate in key meetings;
  • what activities will take place to assure quality; how software configuration management will be implemented and
  • what processes will be used to define, implement and manage the infrastructure.
  • If that is the case, all that is necessarily is to determine the requirements of the method and to participate in the processes as necessary.

Unfortunately, not all approaches to project management provide adequate definition of these essential items. If the processes are not well defined or do not exist, it may be necessary to implement some basic project management techniques in order to protect the OUM implementation from unnecessary risk. While it is critical not to take on an overall project management role when Oracle is not tasked with project management responsibility, managing the work of the Oracle team using basic techniques defined in OUM Manage will provide the necessary supplement to the processes and tasks defined in the OUM method.

At a minimum, the project manager should establish a structured method for planning, scheduling and tracking work tasks, provide regular status covering the team’s activities and any risks, issues or problems they face, and define quality process to cover team development activities. Development efforts require a process for software configuration management. If the customer does not have an established process that can be used, it may be possible to implement a process using a low cost or free tool to protect the code developed up to the point that it is no longer the Oracle team’s responsibility. When using blended development, a common approach to software configuration management is recommended. The OUM Manage focus area provides proven techniques, guidance and templates that should be leveraged to make implementation of these basic activities easy and successful.

MULTIPLE DEPLOYMENT SITE/MULTI-PHASE CONSIDERATIONS

Determine if the transition into production needs to occur for all organizations at the same time. If a phased implementation is planned, decide what applications are to be implemented in each phase. This combination of choices presents four scenarios:
  • Deploy all applications for all organizations at the same time.
  • Deploy all applications for different organizations at different times.
  • Deploy subsets of the applications for all organizations at the same time.
  • Deploy subsets of the applications for different organizations at different times.
Consider the following factors when deciding on a single-phase or multiple-phase deployment:
  • Is the organization facing changes from other sectors? Allow time for an organization to absorb major changes before introducing new factors.
  • Is the organizational culture amenable to integrating the new system into its operations? The risk is less if the organization is accustomed to using a common system with related policies and procedures. If business units have been operating independently, the organization must adapt to a cultural change, as well as to the new system.
  • Can the project team adequately execute the project so that there is minimal risk in implementing all applications for all organizations at one time?
  • Can two or more organizations all fit (from a sizing point of view) on the same servers?
  • What is the company's experience with previous system implementations?
  • What is the project team's experience with previous system implementations?
  • Is the application a production that is controlled, or is it a beta release of the software?

To minimize risk, use the pilot implementation technique. Go live with a carefully selected subset of users who are well trained and able to deal with initial problems. Initial success at a pilot site often sets an attitude of success that carries over to the remaining sites.

Develop an interface between applications and the existing system so that transactions may be processed between both systems. This allows the remainder of the organization to use the current system in parallel with the pilot implementation. For example, journal entries could post to the new General Ledger, as well as to the existing General Ledger. This allows consolidated reporting from the current system until all regions are cut over.

Along with risk reduction, phased deployment adds complexity and cost to the project:

  • The project duration is lengthened requiring the project team to remain intact for a longer period of time.
  • The phase overlaps can create peak resource loading problems. You may be starting a new deployment at the end of a previous one. Production migration needs, startup demands, and support requirements are occurring in parallel.
  • Peaks in computer resource utilization may occur during phase overlaps due to multiple application database instances being needed for training, data conversion, and production for the deployment just ending, as well as start up tasks for the next deployment.

USE OF TEAM LEADERS

Depending on the project (e.g., large, complex, etc.), the project manager may chose to designate various members of the project team as team leaders. The team leader plans, directs, and monitors the day-to-day work of the team.  The team leader assigns work packages to the team members and makes sure they understand the requirements.  The team leader is responsible for building team cohesion, motivating the teams, and providing assistance and encouragement to the team members. Each team leader performs the final quality control and quality assurance for the team by reviewing all work products.  The team leader also signs off on team work completion and quality.  Work that is not up to quality standards is returned.  Team leaders review work products from other teams when these work products may impact aspects of the system.  The team leader reports the team's progress to the project manager.

Often the team leader has a set of work products that they are preparing, but in some cases, the project manager will balance the amount of effort because of the dual responsibility and the additional work that comes with the designation of team leader. In other situations the team leader may also be assigned to lead a sub-project within the overall project and have very few work products to produce. It is up to the project manager to distribute the effort accordingly.

Team leaders can be assigned to lead a discipline team (i.e., business analysts, system analyst, testing specialist), this generally occurs on the tasks within the following OUM process groupings, e.g., Business Requirements (RD), Requirements Analysis (RA), Implementation (IM), Testing (TE), Performance Management (PT), Technical Architecture (TA) and Operations and Support (PS). Therefore, depending on the team being led, the team leader would be designated from the applicable role, i.e., business analyst, system analyst, system architect, technical writer, etc. Examples: Business Lead, Business Analyst Lead, Technical Lead, Testing Lead, Performance Management Lead, etc.
