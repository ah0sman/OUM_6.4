ORACLE SUPPORT SERVICES OVERVIEW

INTRODUCTION

Successful management of an Oracle software and system investment from inception through retirement requires a lifecycle management strategy. The lifecycle management of Oracle software requires individuals that understand the technology and have the necessary skills to successfully manage the system throughout the lifecycle. Such knowledge is acquired through both training and experience.

An effective IT organization aligns itself with the business that it supports by delivering an optimal level of service at a justifiable cost. Achieving this level of best practice requires a framework on which the IT organization can build. Standards and methodologies provide repeatable and predictable means of delivering quality. Oracle-specific best practices, combined with industry standard frameworks, augment Oracle technology to deliver consistency and quality across the lifecycle. The Information Technology Infrastructure Library (ITIL) is an example of such an industry standard framework of best practices and is a starting point for defining and optimizing IT processes emphasizing service quality and continuous improvement. ITIL® is a Registered Trade Mark of the Office of Government Commerce in the United Kingdom and other countries.

ITIL was conceived in the late 1980s when the British Central Computer and Telecommunication Agency (CCTA), now called the Office of Government Commerce (OGC), made the determination that there should be a better way for Information Technology to function. The CCTA commissioned a study group to develop a new approach to managing Information Technology. From this group came Version 1 of ITIL, which was known as the Government Information Technology Infrastructure Management, or GITIM. Version 1 of ITIL was significantly different from the present-day version 3. Some of this difference is due to the gradual maturing of ITIL, while some is due to changes in the Information Technology industry.

ITIL V3 SERVICE LIFECYCLE

The ITIL lifecycle consists of 5 phases:

  • Service Strategy
  • Service Design
  • Service Transition
  • Service Operations
  • Continual Service Improvement

Service Strategy provides the framework for the tactical and strategic activities.

Service Design provides a framework for specifying requirements from the perspective of the customer defining how the requirements are fulfilled.

Service Transition manages the transition of new or changed services and IT management processes to production.

Service Operations is concerned with the day-to-day operations of services and IT management processes in support of the business.

Continual Service Improvement underpins all lifecycle phases and interacts with each phase in an ongoing manner asking, “What can we do better?”

The strength of this lifecycle approach is the continual feedback loop that runs through each phase. Feedback ensures that communication between each phase is constant, which provides opportunities for improvement. Continual evaluation and measurement of delivered services helps to validate the value provided, and ensures that the needs of the business are being addressed. Service Design, Service Transition and Service Operations implement the Service Strategy and are progressive phases of the lifecycle that represent change and transformation. Continual Service Improvement represents learning and improvement. Continual Service Improvement helps place and prioritize improvement programs and projects based on strategic objectives.
