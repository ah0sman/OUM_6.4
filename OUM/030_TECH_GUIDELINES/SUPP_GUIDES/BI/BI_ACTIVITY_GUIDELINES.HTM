BUSINESS INTELLIGENCE (BI) SUPPLEMENTAL ACTIVITY GUIDELINES

The Supplemental Activity Guidelines provided below are meant to assist teams in tailoring the workplan for a BI projects. They also provide some additional guidance for performing the OUM tasks and activities when implementing a Business Intelligence system that includes Enterprise Performance Management (EPM) or Business Intelligence Application (BI Apps) products. OUM defines an activity as "a set of tasks related either by topic, dependencies, data, common skills or work products."

Currently, guidance is provided for the following BI areas of focus:

  • BI Applications (also referred to as BI Apps) - These engagements involve the delivery of projects utilizing commercial-off-the-shelf (COTS) business intelligence offerings such as EPM and other BI Applications. These projects also include some amount of custom work to extend the COTS application(s), as necessary. This work is often referred to as custom extensions.
  • Custom BI (also referred to as Cust BI) - These engagements involve custom technological BI solutions that range from large-scale data warehouses to daily operational business intelligence applications, that are tailored to suit the specific needs of an enterprise. They do not include COTS offerings.

You should assume the guidance applies to all BI projects, including custom BI and BI Apps, unless the guidance explicitly refers to custom BI, BI Apps, and in many cases EPM.

Use the BI supplemental activity guidance provided on the left side of this table, in conjunction with the standard OUM activity overview on the right side of the table, in order to understand how BI impacts specific OUM activities.

The business requirements for the BI solution are identified, refined, and prioritized as outlined in the OUM process.

It is essential that the Detail Business and System Objectives (RD.001) task be completed as a part of every project. This task defines the objectives for the BI project.

Business and/or system goals answers "why" the organization needs a particular requirement. Business and System Objectives answer "how" a requirement will help the business realize the goal. There may be multiple objectives that must be met in order for the business to realize its goal(s).

Common BI and EPM Business Goals:

  • The solution must support improved business decision making.
  • The data must represent "one version of the truth."
  • The data (figures) must be consistent regardless of the view.
  • The solution's structures must be adaptable to changing business needs.
  • The data must be secure (making sensitive information accessible by only a named few).

Traceability of Business Objectives to Goals

An example of a many - to - one (business objectives to business goal) relationship:

Business Goal: The data must represent one version of the truth

Supporting Business Objectives:

  • Merge the pertinent data from JDE, Hyperion, Oracle GL and OM
  • Utilize a single source for the product master
  • Apply consistent business rules and calculations for exchange rates

The Develop Glossary (RD.042) task is highly recommended for all Custom BI, EPM, and BI Apps projects. Terms that are used to classify or identify data should be part of the project's glossary. Terms that will be used across business silos or by both the IT organization and the project team should be reviewed and documented.

The project glossary is particularly useful on BI Apps projects, as the meaning of common business terms can be an effective method for eliciting business rules, identifying missing, or redundant data elements and gaining agreement on calculations.

A Use Case Model (RA.023) should be created for EPM and BI Apps projects that require customizations or enhancements to the EPM or BI Apps product software. It is not necessary to model or specify existing product functionality unless doing so adds measurable value. The focus of the effort in this activity should be on modeling use cases to document gaps between the business requirements and the functionality provided by the chosen product set.

For BI Apps projects, there may be little emphasis on use cases at the black box level. Users are keying in data that is used by the system. The exception to this might be for identifying report requirements for displaying the captured data.

The OUM BI view testing approach is based upon four types of testing:

Unit Testing - Testing that occurs when one develops individual components of the solution (covered in this activity).

Integration Testing - Testing that occurs between some components, also known as string testing. Simply makes sure associated components fulfilling a common functionality are working correctly together (not to be confused with System testing).

System Testing - End-to-end testing of the complete solution, which includes running of any batch cycles. Last chance to verify/validate system prior to users testing.

Acceptance Testing - Also known as user acceptance testing. This is where the system is tested to verify it meets the originally agreed upon acceptance criteria.
