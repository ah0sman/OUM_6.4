[SLM] SERVICE LEVEL MANAGEMENT

From an ITIL perspective, the purpose of the Service Level Management process is to "ensure that all current and planned IT services are delivered to agreed achievable targets."1

Back to Top

OBJECTIVES

From an ITIL perspective, the objectives of the Service Level Management process include1:

  • "Defining, documenting, agreeing, monitoring, measuring, reporting, and reviewing the level of IT services provided."
  • "Improving the relationship and communication with the business and the customers."
  • "Ensuring that specific and measurable targets are developed."
  • "Monitoring and improving customer satisfaction."
  • "Ensuring a clear and unambiguous expectation of the level of service to be delivered."
  • "Ensuring continual improvement to the levels of service, even when all agreed targets are met."

Back to Top

APPROACH

Not all Oracle service contracts include a Service Level Agreement (SLA). An SLA may include service level targets for availability, incident management, operational events (i.e. refreshes), IT service continuity management, and other events or metrics. An SLA may also include service level targets for business transaction or process completion times.

For customer contracts that include an SLA, close attention should be paid to the SLA Exclusions section, which may detail conditions under which Oracle's obligation to perform per the SLA may be excused. For example, some SLA targets, such as availability and incident resolution time, may apply only for services in production status. For an SLA with service level targets for business transactions or processes, full implementation of these targets may require a collaborative effort between Oracle and the customer to define which business transactions and/or processes should be monitored, to monitor these targets for purposes of establishing baselines, and to agree on a schedule to implement these service level targets in production. Even for services in production status, a temporary suspension of the SLA may apply after go live or after a major service change to allow for a specified stabilization period. Customers should also review the contract sections for Warranty and Exclusive Remedy; these sections describe what recourse customers have, and the process to follow, in the event that customers seek service level credits.

Customers should consult with their Service Delivery Manager (SDM) and/or Transition Manager (TM) to ensure a full and accurate understanding of how service level management works for their contract, including how to fully implement an SLA and what exclusions apply.

Performance against the SLA may depend on third party suppliers and/or coordination between Oracle teams, and any underpinning contracts or operational level agreements (OLA) are confidential and not subject to external disclosure.

Contracts with an SLA typically include periodic reporting deliverables that provide details about performance against the SLA. Oracle Pulse is also available for Oracle customers to use mobile devices to monitor service level targets such as availability. It is highly recommended that customers include review and discussion of performance against the SLA as a critical component of service governance and business relationship management with Oracle as a means to review and discuss customer satisfaction relative to performance against the SLA, to most effectively correct service deficiencies that impact expected service levels, and to drive continual service improvement.
