[CHM] CHANGE MANAGEMENT

From an ITIL perspective, "the purpose of the Change Management process is to control the lifecycle of all changes, enabling beneficial changes to be made with minimum disruption to the IT services”.1

Back to Top

OBJECTIVES

From an ITIL perspective, the objectives of the Change Management process include1:

  • “Responding to changing business requirements while maximizing value and reducing incidents, disruption and re-work”.
  • “Responding to RFCs that will align services with business needs”.
  • “Ensuring that changes are recorded and evaluated, and that authorized changes are prioritized, planned, tested, implemented, documented and reviewed in a controlled manner”.
  • “Optimizing overall business risk”.

Back to Top

APPROACH

For service changes that Oracle introduces across all customers, including scheduled maintenance for shared (shared across multiple customers) data center infrastructure that is performed on a rolling basis throughout the year, changes are coordinated and planned through the SDM and/or TM. Inputs for such changes include:

  • Service requirements from the customer.
  • Changes to service level agreements between Oracle and customers.
  • Requirements from the Service Asset and Configuration process.
  • Changes to Oracle business objectives.
  • Feedback from the Oracle Continual Service Improvement process.

For service changes that impact a single customer, these changes may be driven by either Oracle (i.e. technical/infrastructure changes) or the customer. For customer-requested changes, it is important to first understand whether the change is included in the contract. Many changes require a contract amendment and as such may involve the Oracle sales team. For service changes that do not require a contract amendment, it is highly recommended that the internal customer change advisory board (CAB), including the emergency CAB include Oracle resources. The Oracle Service Plan, which is typically a governance deliverable that applies for certain contracts (see also Business Relationship Management), should serve as the basis to document all approved, planned and scheduled service changes (see also Release and Deployment Management, Change Evaluation, Service Validation and Testing).

When executing service changes, the Request for Change (RFC) entered in My Oracle Support (MOS) is typically the mechanism for scheduling and documenting the details of what is being requested. By policy, Oracle must successfully execute a change in a non-production environment before a change can be promoted to production. Additionally, changes to production can only be submitted for scheduling by an authorized approver. It is highly recommended that customers perform both regression and performance testing on changes as appropriate.

Exceptions for use of MOS are made for certain Extended Services customers, including those customers who purchase the Extended Service which allows for use of a customer’s technical assistance system.
