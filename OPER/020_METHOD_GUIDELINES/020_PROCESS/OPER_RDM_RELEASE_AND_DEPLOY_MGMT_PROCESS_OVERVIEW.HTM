[RDM] RELEASE AND DEPLOYMENT MANAGEMENT

From an ITIL perspective, "the purpose of the release and deployment management process is to plan, schedule and control the build, test, and deployment of releases, and to deliver new functionality required by the business while protecting the integrity of existing services".1

Back to Top

OBJECTIVES

From an ITIL perspective, objectives of the Release and Deployment Management process include:1

  • "Defining and agreeing on release and deployment management plans".
  • "Creating and testing release packages stored in a definitive media library (DML) and recorded in the configuration management system (CMS)".
  • "Deploying release packages from the DML following the agreed plan".
  • Ensuring that organization and stakeholder changes are managed".>
  • "Ensuring that the new or changed service can deliver the agreed utility and warranty".
  • "Ensuring that there is knowledge transfer to customers, users, and IT".

APPROACH

In the context of Oracle service engagements specific to individual customers, the Service Plan, which is typically a governance deliverable that applies for certain contracts (see also Business Relationship Management), should serve as the basis to for scheduling and planning all releases, including minor, major, and emergency releases. Regardless of whether a Service Plan is contractually obligated, it is highly recommended that all Oracle engagements implement a Service Plan. It is also highly recommended that customers proactively define, in coordination with the SDM and/or TM, when (i.e. monthly, etc.) scheduled maintenance windows are allowed by end users and the duration (i.e. hours) of these scheduled maintenance windows. Release planning should consider the maximum scheduled downtime allowed by end users, as well as all key business events (i.e. month/quarter/year-end close, payroll, benefit enrollment period, etc.).

It is imperative that any Oracle software release requested by a customer be reviewed (with input by both Oracle and customer) for supportability by Oracle and any relevant third parties.

Release planning should also consider input from the Oracle Periodic Maintenance Program (PMP). The PMP applies for certain contracts, and includes patching for technology updates and implementation of system assessment recommendations by Oracle teams. Customers should review their contract or consult with their SDM or TM to determine the extent to which a PMP applies to their contract.

To deploy service releases, the Request for Change (RFC) entered in My Oracle Support (MOS) is typically the mechanism for scheduling and documenting the details of what is being requested. By policy, Oracle must successfully execute an RFC in a non-production environment before a change can be promoted to production. Additionally, changes to production can only be submitted for scheduling by an authorized approver. It is highly recommended that customers perform both regression and performance testing on changes as appropriate (see also Change Evaluation, Service Validation and Testing).

Exceptions for use of MOS are made for certain Extended Services customers, including those customers who purchase the Extended Service which allows for use of a customer’s technical assistance system.

Entitlements and roles and responsibilities for the release and deployment management process are defined in Oracle service schedules. It is highly recommended that all customers review contracts and these schedules to understand the scope of what is included in their contract and their responsibilities per the contract.

Another important release and deployment management consideration is the extent to which major releases or upgrades are included or not in contracts. Typically, major releases or upgrades are not included, and are subject to additional fees and a contract amendment. On this question, it is important to engage with the SDM and/or TM who can engage the Oracle sales and solution teams as needed.
